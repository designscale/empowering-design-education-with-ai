Chapter 4: The Creative Industry

“AI will not replace designers; designers who use AI will replace designers who don’t.” C.J. Yeh

Every chapter so far has made its case from inside the classroom. This one steps outside it.

Over the past several weeks we sat down with people who are not academics: a founder who built a company on AI-native workflows after fifteen years in strategy at ByteDance, the CEO of a generative tool used by hundreds of creative teams, a studio principal who has been running a brand practice in Brooklyn since 2004, a creative director whose entire specialty is generative film for brands too cautious to say the word “AI” out loud a year ago, a product designer who runs AI education for five hundred designers at Meta, a filmmaker at Google Creative Lab who is one of the few artists in the world actively developing Google’s next generation of creative tools, a UX design lead at Google DeepMind who spends her days shaping how a generative model behaves, an advisory visual designer inside IBM’s own CIO organization who builds the AI-powered tools IBM’s employees use every day, a chief creative officer building campaigns for Google and Peloton out of studios in Sydney, Los Angeles, and New York, and a head of design at Notion who also chairs a graduate design program at the School of Visual Arts. We asked each of them some version of the same five questions: what’s actually changing, what they’re hiring for, what they wish design schools were teaching, where the human contribution still lives, and what gives them hope.

What follows are those conversations, presented one at a time, in each person’s own words.

Randy Hunt

Head of Design, Notion | Chair, MFA Design, School of Visual Arts

Full excerpt: Interviews/Randy_Hunt_Excerpt.md | Interviewed July 20, 2026

Randy Hunt is Head of Design at Notion and chairs the MFA Design program at the School of Visual Arts. He’s spent more than twenty years designing and building multi-sided marketplaces: Supermarket, a designer-made marketplace he co-founded, followed by Etsy, Artsy, and Grab, Southeast Asia’s super-app. Earlier, he founded and directed the design studio Citizen Scholar Inc., working with clients across education, environment, arts, and social good, and he’s the author of Product Design for the Web (New Riders, 2013). Randy runs design at a fast-moving product company while chairing a graduate design program at the same time, and that’s exactly why we wanted to talk to him: his answers move constantly between what’s shipping at Notion right now and what he’s seeing shift in the classroom at SVA.

What They’re Seeing

Our question to Randy was how AI is fundamentally transforming the design process, not just the tools but the way creative decisions get made. He answered through the Notion lens. “It’s faster, but I don’t mean it’s faster in the sense that we’re necessarily shipping to customers faster, although maybe we are,” he said. “What I mean is that designers are more quickly able to express their design ideas at higher fidelity, and therefore get richer feedback on those ideas sooner, and that accelerates the process.” The other shift he named was access: a lot of what Notion’s design team needs, customer conversations, support tickets, sales calls, past research, already lives inside Notion itself, and AI tools make that history far easier to query and follow up on. “We’re not necessarily having to spin up a unique new research project anytime we want some customer insight,” he said. It hasn’t replaced research entirely, but it’s made it much faster to check whether an intuition is really a problem worth solving.

That speed has changed how decisions get made, too. “We probably have a stronger appetite to just quickly make decisions and move forward,” he said, pointing to the confidence that comes from better insight and higher design fidelity earlier in the process. He named something more subtle underneath that: because it’s easier to make things, it also feels easier to redo them, so a decision doesn’t feel as heavy or as expensive to walk back later. “There’s just more forward momentum,” he said, though he was careful to note his team still moves with more caution wherever a misstep could genuinely hurt a customer.

Randy’s clearest example of how this actually plays out was a story about friction, not speed. Notion’s designers were already technical before any of this, already shipping changes to production code because they had the skills, not because a new AI tool suddenly made it possible. A few months ago, one of his designers was unhappy with small UI details, alignment, text size, the pace of an animation, that didn’t match the original design intent. Engineering estimated a few weeks to fix it, so he spent a weekend doing it himself, beautifully. But it came back as one enormous pull request. “Engineering was like, this is amazing, but now it’s going to take us two weeks to review this,” Randy said, because none of it had been broken into the kind of small, reviewable pieces that make code review efficient. “We kind of go through these cycles,” he said. “Real efficiency in one part of the process, and some other inefficiency shows up on the other side that we didn’t anticipate.”

What They’re Hiring For

Randy told us it still feels early to say what’s disappearing from candidates. “I’m not sure yet,” he said. “It doesn’t seem like there’s something lacking. I do wonder, as we talk to more designers who are AI-native, what that starts to feel like over time.” What he does look for, in two specific ways, is fluency. The first is whether someone has genuinely incorporated AI tools into their own process and can talk through the judgment calls behind which tool they used and why, since that judgment keeps shifting month to month. “We can usually see that the way they did something nine months ago is different from six months ago, which is different from three months ago,” he said. “Seeing that change is a good sign. It says they’re genuinely engaged, trying new things, curious.”

The second, rarer signal is whether a candidate has actually designed an AI experience, not just used AI tools to design faster. He described interviewing a young designer who’d worked on a support tool for doctors’ clinics, essentially phone support reimagined, where the real design problem was building the rule system: letting each clinic define its own policies so an LLM could answer routine questions while knowing exactly what it wasn’t allowed to touch for compliance reasons. “The design proposal only exists because the technology exists,” Randy said. “It’s not just that AI tools were used to make it. The concept itself was only possible because of the technology.” That it came from an early-career designer stuck with him. “I found that really encouraging.”

What They Wish Schools Were Teaching

Randy’s answer circled around one word: literacy. Not tool training, but a real understanding of what these systems are, what they’re good and not good at, and how to weigh using them at all. “I think very soon these tools won’t feel free anymore, and that will change people’s relationship to them,” he said, pointing to the commercial, social, and environmental costs that rarely make it into a syllabus. “A lot of teaching right now is focused on the new thing because it’s exciting, or because it feels like the job market demands it. I think literacy around the broader topic is where the real critical thinking comes in, and where the longer-term value is.”

He offered two student examples that stuck with him. One student, with no background in software, became fascinated with the fact that typefaces are essentially code: weights, dynamic behavior, all of it scripted underneath the visual craft. Using AI coding tools, the student taught themselves how fonts are actually built and started executing real typefaces that way. “I loved that,” Randy said. Another student spent a summer helping with a family warehouse business in India and ended up designing and building a resource-planning application for it, entirely from what they’d learned in his class. “That one’s a much more practical, immediate application,” he said, “and I really love seeing that too.”

The Human Layer

Randy named three tells for telling apart a designer who’s curating and orchestrating AI work from one who’s just shipping whatever it produces. The first is dialogue. “I’ve seen students come in with work and reference something in the output, and I’ll ask, why did you choose this? And they’ll say, I don’t know,” he said. That’s not automatically a problem, using the tool is fine, but not being able to explain your own output is. “The critical thinking version is engaging with what it actually said: I don’t know what this means, let me understand it, why did it say this. There’s a level of inquiry that builds real understanding.”

The second is breadth. “It’s not just one output, one thing,” he said. “It’s, let me try it this way, I get a different result, let me try it this way too.” Seeing the permutations tells him there was a real process behind the final piece, not a single prompt.

The third is harder to name but easy to feel, a kind of pattern recognition designers develop over time. “Today, I can go to a website and pretty quickly sense someone made this with a particular AI tool, because there’s a certain aesthetic, a set of preferences, a way the type gets handled,” he said. He compared it to something he noticed fifteen years ago, when a design-education case-study format popularized by General Assembly became so common that designers stopped doing the underlying work and just filled in the template. “The moment I saw a case study like that, I knew I couldn’t really talk to that designer about it, because I didn’t trust that they understood what was behind it,” he said. “I think the same thing is happening now. When something looks like everything else, it tells me they’re likely just shipping whatever it produced.”

What Gives Them Hope / The Future

Randy’s picture of who matters most going into 2027 came down to three things. “Being extremely curious, both about what’s happening in the world and about your own process, curious enough to be willing to change it,” he said, and applying that curiosity aggressively, “willing to replace your own knowledge quickly,” even if what you learned yesterday felt like exactly what you needed. “It’s not about knowing the most. It’s about learning the quickest, having a habit and a ritual of continuous learning.” The counterpoint, he was quick to add, is that pure design fundamentals haven’t gone anywhere. “Understanding good typography, information hierarchy, how people relate to color and symbolism, these are hard to quantify, but when everyone can make a lot of things easily, it becomes much more valuable to be the person who can make the thing people actually remember.”

Asked what he’d say directly to educators, including himself, Randy didn’t separate himself from the advice. “We probably need to unlearn as much as we need to learn,” he said. “A lot of the methods and techniques that got us here aren’t necessarily the ones that carry us into the next chapter. Educators need to stay in a student mindset too.”

Kavin Kapoor

Filmmaker, Google Creative Lab

Full excerpt: Interviews/Kavin_Kapoor_Excerpt.md | Interviewed July 15, 2026

Kavin Kapoor is a filmmaker and creative researcher at Google Creative Lab, working at the intersection of art and technology. His work has ranged from AAA video games at PlayStation to AR experiences at Meta to short films at Tribeca, and has led him to collaborate with directors including Darren Aronofsky and Hideo Kojima. He’s currently one of the few artists in the world actively developing Google’s next generation of creative tools, and is in active research on Veo, Google’s generative video model. Outside of that, he travels the world leading workshops and mentoring artists, and volunteers as a Flow Sessions mentor, a guest lecturer at USC and Chapman, and a member of the Runway Creative Partners Program. We wanted his perspective because he sits inside the company building the tools the rest of this chapter’s interviewees are reacting to, and because he moves fluidly between studio production, tool research, and teaching, rather than occupying just one of those seats.

What They’re Seeing

Kavin’s answer to how AI is transforming the design process started with a bar it’s raised, not lowered. “AI has raised the bar for what the baseline expectation is during the design process,” he said. The clearest example is inside his own studio’s pitch meetings: it’s no longer enough to bring a slide with bullet points. The expectation now is a working prototype, something that shows what an idea would actually look like in front of an audience, whether the work is a two-dimensional poster or a film. Commercials, he said, are increasingly previsualized in full with AI before anything is shot. There’s an old term in the film industry for this: do it in pre. “We’re asking questions a lot earlier than we ever had before,” he said, “is this the right lighting, is this the right costume. Those used to be things you didn’t have to answer until you were on set.”

He was careful to name the cost that comes with the speed. AI is a superpower that lets his team work more efficiently, but on tight deadlines, that superpower cuts both ways: it’s easier to make things quickly, which also means the expectation shifts to making them later. TV deadlines used to be fixed six weeks out, to leave time for editing and sound, with no way to reshoot. “Now you can reshoot any sequence up until the morning of,” he said. “I don’t think that’s necessarily the best thing, but it does help, sometimes.”

What They’re Hiring For

Kavin hasn’t watched a skill disappear yet, by his own account, and he doesn’t think it’s been long enough to see one go. What he has watched is the workforce loosen at the edges: AI now lets a designer end up a filmmaker, or a technologist end up a designer, and he loves that shift because it rewards someone who’s curious about a lot of things rather than one thing done narrowly. The clearest signal that a candidate actually understands how to direct AI, he said, is that they aren’t calling themselves an AI designer or “AI forward.” Understanding your relationship to AI as a tool is what matters most to him when he’s hiring, especially as a filmmaker himself. “When someone tells me they’re an AI filmmaker, that actually worries me more than someone who says they’re anti-AI completely,” he said. What he’s really listening for is curiosity. “The strongest people in the workforce working with AI are the ones asking the most creative questions, because the best prompts are the best questions, the ones that come from instinct. You don’t necessarily have to be the best designer, but you have to be the designer thinking about the best questions.”

What They Wish Schools Were Teaching

Kavin has taught at a string of film-focused programs, USC, Chapman, LMU, and named the same gap at all of them: none of them talk about where these tools are actually going. “We live in a world now where it’s very hard to draw a fence around it and say, if you’re an artist or a creative, you don’t have to worry about technology,” he said. Technologists, in his experience, are entering the creative space faster and more comfortably than fine artists are entering technology, and the fine artists who do make that leap “stand out leagues ahead.” His advice to schools was direct: teach students about where technology is headed and how to coexist with it, because that fluency is what lets them define their own path.

The student work that’s surprised him most, he said, always intersects with something beyond art or design in a way that hasn’t been done before, echoing a phrase used inside Google’s own studio: do it in a way that’s never been done before. “I think the next big thing is going to be made by someone who understands both tech and art,” he said, “not just an artist.”

The Human Layer

Asked how he tells apart a designer who’s curating and orchestrating AI work from one who’s just shipping whatever it produces, Kavin went past the obvious tells, the chat typos, the stuff anyone can spot, straight to intention. “If you’re a creative person using AI, you have to be able to justify it,” he said. “Ask yourself why you’re using it for this.” A good answer might be that it would have taken too long, or cost too much, or unlocked an art style that wasn’t possible before. He offered his own current project as an example: a film that’s almost entirely live action, no AI, except for one stadium sequence that needs over a thousand people in the crowd, a case where AI is a genuinely good solution. “That’s what curating and orchestrating looks like,” he said. “There’s real intention behind the usage. If you’re proud of how you used it, you should be able to justify it and explain why. The people who are cagey about what parts are AI are usually the ones just shipping it.”

What Gives Them Hope / The Future

Kavin’s picture of who matters most going forward wasn’t about tools at all, it was about range. “What I think will be most valuable are designers who consume other art beyond their own field,” he said, pointing to how boxed-in genres used to be simply because it took so much effort to learn something new, a barrier that’s mostly gone now. “Designers who only do one thing aren’t as future-proof.” His advice for a student building a career was to lean into every interest and learn to bridge them, something he called “arranging and organizing things.” Every designer he’s worked with at Google had at least one other interest running alongside their design work, motion design and music, motion design and fashion, and he pointed to his own instinct as a filmmaker who still notices a well-made piece of clothing and thinks, what an interesting idea. “Fashion drives a lot of design innovation,” he said, “and I think that’s especially true when we’re talking about a place like FIT.”

His message to educators circled back to the same point. “Encourage students to try out all of their other interests,” he said. “Bridging ideas, being interdisciplinary, that’s a strength now, not a sign someone can’t decide on one thing. This is the era where having multiple interests is what makes you different and makes your voice stronger.”

Eunjoo Kim

Sr. Staff UX Design Lead, Google DeepMind

Full excerpt: Interviews/Eunju_Kim_Excerpt.md | Interviewed [DATE TBD]

Eunjoo Kim is a Senior Staff UX Design Lead at Google DeepMind, where she drives the user experience for Gemini’s multimodal conversational AI across a growing device ecosystem: XR glasses, wearables, cars, and more. She brings more than twenty-five years of UX design and strategy experience to that work, having led innovation at Samsung, Motorola, Qualcomm, and Google. She was shortlisted as one of the top 18 women leading the way in wearable tech and VR in 2016, and named a top 50 wearable tech game changer by Wearable magazine. What came through most is how much of her day-to-day work involves training how a generative model should behave: what tone counts as polite, what makes people push back. We wanted her perspective because so much of the rest of this chapter comes from people building around AI. Eunjoo’s team is helping build the thing itself.

What They’re Seeing

Eunjoo’s answer to how AI is transforming the design process started from inside her own organization. Because her team builds AI products, she said, the nature of what needs to be designed has changed, and that’s changed how designers approach the work. “The biggest difference is that it has a non-deterministic aspect,” she said. “In traditional design, you’d plan everything out completely in advance, almost like saying: this dish should be eaten this way, made from these ingredients, cooked this way, served in this setting, while this music plays, and then you hand over a perfectly finished dish. Gen AI flips that. You’re designing something without knowing what the output will actually be, which means defining an indirect experience instead of a direct one. That requires a real shift in mindset.” In practice, her team ends up training how the model should behave: what tone to use in a given situation, what not to say, what reads as impolite. “You teach the model, and sometimes you get the answer you want, and sometimes you don’t,” she said. Early on, not being able to fully control the output was uncomfortable. Now, she said, there’s a much more natural acceptance of that, which has changed how her team ships: instead of testing every edge case before launch the way a traditional product would, they release once something’s been reasonably tested internally, watch how the market responds, and tune from there, on a cadence some AI products repeat every three or four months.

The other shift she named was about the interaction itself. Her products are conversational by default, and conversational UX, she said, is a genuinely difficult UX. “There are so many users who don’t know how to talk to the system,” she said. We haven’t reached AGI (Artificial General Intelligence, an AI with the flexible, human-like reasoning to understand context the way a person would across any situation), so the system can’t fully read what a user means, and most users don’t know how to give it the context it needs to fill that gap. There’s also a habit she notices especially in younger users: they’ve grown so used to browsing and consuming that co-creating with a product instead feels genuinely hard. With something like YouTube, she pointed out, the experience barely changes between a novice and an advanced user. With a generative product, the gap between what a skilled user gets out of it and what an average user gets out of it can be enormous. “How do you close that gap?” she said, “is one of the biggest open questions we have.”

She also described how much faster the actual work has gotten: last year, the conversation on her team was about velocity; this year, it’s about doing ten times the work in a fraction of the time, with agents doing a real share of it, and roles have blurred along with the speed. Designers vibe-code working prototypes themselves now, sometimes straight into production. Her team has started building its own tools to speed up testing and research: UX audits that used to mean asking an engineer for help are now automated by designers who build the tool themselves, and UX research that used to take two weeks to turn into a report now takes about two days.

What They’re Hiring For

Eunjoo has watched workforce restructuring unfold on her own team for the past two years, probably one of the first waves to hit the industry. An experienced designer working the traditional way can still match the speed of an early-career designer leaning on AI that isn’t very refined yet, so raw speed alone doesn’t tell her as much as it used to. What tells her more is attitude, not tenure, no matter how many years someone’s put in, even when their immediate result still looks fine. The people actually pulling ahead are the ones leading from the front: trying something, sharing what happened, and building a reputation for it.

Where someone went to school or which companies they’ve worked at isn’t something she weighs much anymore, she said. Everyone she interviews now claims to be an “AI” designer or “AI” PM, but when she digs in, it’s often a machine learning project from two or three years ago, which in this domain already reads as ancient history. What she weighs far more heavily than a school name, a title, or years of experience is how much AI proficiency a candidate would actually bring on day one. “Gen AI’s history is so short that even a short amount of real experience with it isn’t a downside anymore,” she said. “It can look like a bigger asset than a decade of experience with something that no longer applies.”

The Human Layer

Someone once told Eunjoo that AI’s performance is the performance of your own intelligence, and she thinks that’s exactly right. Two things follow from it, and they’re different from each other. The first is the ability to evaluate what AI generates, which she thinks matters more now than it ever has. Because most generative tools produce fairly similar output, being able to use your own taste and your own point of view to spot what’s slightly off, or what’s missing, or what needs to be different, is an evaluative skill more important than ever. Without it, she said, “you’d end up presenting a very generic, AI-typical output as if it were your own.”

The second is the ability to direct multiple tools toward one result rather than living inside a single one. She was back in the kitchen again: you need to know where the ingredients you need actually live and how to source them, whether that’s one tool for image, another for sound, another for code, to put together something genuinely good. In an agent-driven workflow, she said, how many agents you can lead, and how well, changes the output itself, not just how fast you can produce it. “That changes the speed, the volume, and the overall impact of your contribution,” she said. “The role a human chooses to play has an enormous effect on what comes out the other end.”

What Gives Them Hope / The Future

Eunjoo’s closing thought was about timing. She still thinks of this as the early, golden window of the field, which means students showing up with little or no experience aren’t at the disadvantage they would have been a few years ago. In the past, design skill was something closer to a trained craftsmanship, built up slowly through repetition. With AI, what matters most is how recently and how much someone has actually used the newest tools, which puts a student who graduated last month on more even footing with someone who’s been working for a decade. She was direct about what that means practically. With how many solopreneurs and one-person companies exist now, “I couldn’t get a job” isn’t really a valid excuse anymore, in her view, and there’s no longer a real barrier between building something and getting it in front of users. Her advice was to shift away from the old mindset that waits for someone else to hand you an opportunity. “You should think of yourself as the CEO and start your own thing right away,” she said, “and once that builds up, you can connect it directly to your career.”

Karen Lin

Product Designer, Meta

Full excerpt: Interviews/Karen_Lin_Excerpt.md | Interviewed July 2, 2026

Karen Lin is a Product Designer at Meta, where she designs social experiences for billions of people across Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, and more. In parallel, she leads AI education for a 500-person design org. Before this, she built design systems at Atlassian, and before that, she was an architect who designed skyscrapers. She grew up in Australia and now calls New York home. Because she does this at one of the largest design organizations in the industry, her perspective carries the weight of watching hundreds of designers adopt these tools in real time, not just her own workflow.

What They’re Seeing

Karen’s read on how AI is transforming the design process started with a culture shift she’s watched unfold inside Meta itself. “Prototyping culture is a lot stronger now,” she said. “We used to rely on this internal tool with a steep learning curve, and a lot of us struggled with it, or never really picked it up. With vibe coding and AI, prototyping has become so easy that we’re seeing PMs, engineers, researchers, even managers and leaders building things. Which is so inspiring. It used to be mostly designers who built and shared ideas. Now everyone is empowered to, and the design process doesn’t just belong to designers anymore.”

The other shift she named was about fidelity. “I used to polish my designs for ages before showing anyone,” she said. “Now I’ll quickly vibe-code a rough sketch and show it to my cross-functional partners early. Only after we’re aligned, I progress to high fidelity, instead of trying to build the perfect prototype on the first try. Saves a lot of time and effort.”

What They’re Hiring For

Karen’s hiring bar has less to do with whether someone uses AI and more to do with how they use it. “There’s always a quality bar I check,” she said. “AI isn’t perfect, it hallucinates, and I definitely notice when someone doesn’t review their work after AI produces it. If someone understands how to direct AI, their work still uses it, but it’s high quality, and they’re often working at a higher velocity because of it.” She pays close attention to how people prompt. “An example of a weak prompt is: here’s a problem, give me the answer. A good designer prompts something more like: give me several options, critique yourself, weigh the pros and cons, then recommend the best one and explain why. That’s the next level of thinking.”

She leads education for her design org of 500 people by scaling the work of her immediate team of 9 designers, who prototype and ship code directly into production, instead of using traditional design tools. “If I were hiring a designer today, I’d be looking for someone who is comfortable shipping code,” she said. “That doesn’t mean they need to be great at it immediately, but they need to show proactivity, curiosity, and a willingness to try new tools. To me, there’s a world of difference between a hypothetical mockup and a real shipped product.”

What They Wish Schools Were Teaching

This is where Karen got the most animated. “I have strong opinions about this. Design school should teach people how to have good taste,” she said. “AI can spit out generic things, but it’s a designer’s job to set the quality bar. Someone with good taste can pick out the things that everyone looks at and says, ‘That’s objectively beautiful.’ In a world where machines can generate infinite iterations, the most valuable designers are the ones who can curate.” She’s genuinely wrestled with whether taste is something you’re born with or something you can train, and she lands firmly on the side of training it. “With taste, you can fake-it-till-you-make-it. When I’m designing something I don’t know much about, I go out and find inspiration: an art gallery, a Pinterest board, a person on social media who is an expert in this field, and I study the design decisions they make. Think of it as training. Imitating people with good taste is how you can start to develop your own.”

She lit up, describing a student night of Christie’s that had genuinely surprised her. “I’ve spent many years in corporate design, working on products where the stakes are high and the craft is precise: enterprise software, growth, monetization, etc. These products are more serious than they are playful. So many of the student portfolios I saw were genuinely delightful. They sparked joy. I’d land on one and think, ‘This is so fun, I’m intrigued, I want to explore more.’ I miss that element of play. Somewhere in all those years of professional work, I’d lost touch with the playful creativity we start with in school. The students wear it so openly, and I think it’s worth all of us holding onto it.”

The Human Layer

On telling apart a designer who’s curating and orchestrating AI work from one who’s just shipping whatever it produces, Karen said the tell is the same one she uses in hiring. “If you’re curating and orchestrating, the work has higher craft quality. If someone is just shipping whatever it produces, I catch it right away: generic designs, small mistakes, hallucinations, things that weren’t thoughtfully put together.”

What Gives Them Hope / The Future

Karen’s picture of who matters most in 2027 kept circling back to curiosity. “You don’t have to be great at any of it from day one, but you do have to have an open mind and the willingness to evolve with the industry. The designers who lean into that curiosity, who step outside their comfort zone when something new shows up, are the ones I’d bet on.” She brought up a quote from Jensen Huang that stuck with her: the most intelligent people are the ones who can see around corners and preempt challenges before they show up. “Nobody can predict the future,” she said, “but the people with the intuition to guess where things are heading are the ones who’ll be most valuable.”

Her advice for a student building a career in a field moving this fast was refreshingly practical. “Learn to prompt well, and how to collaborate effectively with AI. Because no matter what tool you’re using, you’ll need those skills,” she said. She has reassured her own team that the pace wasn’t as overwhelming as it felt. “Most of these tools can be grouped into similar buckets. If you learn to vibe code in one type of tool, such as Claude Code, you can pick up other terminal tools, such as Codex or Gemini CLI, just as fast. Same with code editors, design applications, and so on.”

When we asked what she’d say to design educators, her answer surprised us by being about people, not tools. “The human touch: knowing when to use AI, and just as importantly, when not to. We use AI constantly at work, but if you’re trying to reach alignment with stakeholders on a politically sensitive decision, an AI-drafted message isn’t always the right move. What actually gets your design shipped is human connection and authenticity. Not because your AI bot sent them a solution to approve, but because you built a genuine relationship, and they like and trust you as a person.”

Weber Wong

Founder & CEO, FLORA

Full excerpt: Interviews/Weber_Wong_Excerpt.md | Interviewed July 1, 2026

Weber Wong is the Founder & CEO of FLORA, a professional creative tool that brings the best generative models together onto one infinite canvas. Before founding it, he was a graduate student at NYU’s ITP, a master’s program focused on creative technology. Talking with Weber felt different from the other interviews in this chapter. He isn’t just using generative tools, he built one, and he’s watched hundreds of creative teams adopt it in real time. We wanted his read on what’s actually changing, from someone standing at the center of the shift rather than downstream of it.

What They’re Seeing

Our first question for him was how AI is fundamentally transforming the design process, not just the tools but the way creative decisions get made. He didn’t answer with a hot take, he answered with history. “Adobe was founded in the 1980s to build creative interfaces for the personal computing paradigm: controlling the graphical user interface, controlling individual pixels, to make one piece of media at a time,” he said. “Generative tools are fundamentally different. It’s top down. You ask for what you want, you get a rough eighty percent version, and then you chisel away from there to get exactly what you want. In some ways that’s actually more natural. It’s more like carving: you move away the big chunks, then chisel toward the fine details.”

We wanted to know what that looks like in practice, inside his own studio. He walked us through the whole arc of a creative project: an idea, divergent exploration, a convergent decision, then production and refinement. “Traditionally, that first exploration step is enormously time intensive,” he told us. “One of our first customers, a designer at Pentagram, used to get asked to explore hundreds of variations of a typeface by hand, in Illustrator. It would take days. Now she can generate hundreds of variants, choose the one that’s conceptually right with her creative director, and refine from there.” He lit up describing how that process can now be codified into something repeatable, an entire workflow that reflects a person’s own creative judgment, reusable with infinite variation. “It lets creatives go from zero to one much faster, and once they know what they want, from one to a thousand,” he said. “I think generative tools will make the world more beautiful, because it takes less creative labor to get an idea into the world, and that means more people can drive their own ideas forward instead of just executing someone else’s.”

What They’re Hiring For

What tells him a designer understands how to direct AI, rather than just accepting whatever it hands them? We put that to him directly. “What’s valuable today is taste, judgment, direction, the ability to look at a creative problem and piece together visual, cultural, and other references into an effective solution,” he said. Execution, he added, is a different story. “I do think the value of creative execution is going down. Across the industry, I’d say eighty to ninety percent of creative time goes to executing an idea rather than figuring out what it should be. In ten or twenty years, people will look back at how much time we spent learning what one button does versus another and be amazed.”

He compared it to disruptions he’d seen before, horses giving way to cars, and offered a smaller, more personal one: he throws pottery, and most people now own mass-produced plates that are genuinely good, even as there are fewer village potters left. “I think the shift is from taste-plus-execution to taste-plus-the-ability-to-build-creative-systems,” he said. He was careful to note that knowing the tools well isn’t the same as having judgment. “There’s an emerging class of AI-forward agencies who mostly get paid for a temporary information advantage, not for deeper creative direction. I think everyone catches up to the tools eventually. What has always mattered is having great ideas and being able to execute them well as a team.”

What They Wish Schools Were Teaching

Weber didn’t hesitate when we asked what he thought design schools should be doing differently. “I think what you two are doing is pretty spot on,” he said. “Traditional education is still necessary, if not more so. I took a class at ITP called Visual Language, and if I hadn’t taken it, I wouldn’t know what good composition even looks like.” What changes, he said, is the tool underneath the thinking, not the thinking itself. “When we taught style transfer in Flora, to do it well you actually have to articulate what you want in a way that’s closer to how you’d think in a visual language class, not further from it.”

The Human Layer

How does he tell apart a designer who’s genuinely curating AI work from one who’s just shipping whatever it produces? “You can tell,” he said simply. “I don’t think a piece of work is inherently better because someone added a Photoshop layer, and I don’t think it’s inherently worse because it was AI-generated. I don’t think AI is a new medium, exactly. It’s a new method. What humans find compelling stays the same.” Then he told me something one of his own customers keeps asking him: can Flora build a model to judge whether something is on brand? His answer says everything about where he thinks the real work still lives. “I always think, isn’t that your job? What does on-brand even mean? That’s the point of having a person there.”

What Gives Them Hope / The Future

For the last stretch, we wanted his take on what kind of designer will matter most in 2027, and what he’d tell a student graduating today. “The designers who matter most are the ones with taste, plus AI literacy, plus enough curiosity and ambition to keep learning in a moment that’s changing this fast,” he said. He was honest about the uncertainty in that answer too. “It’s genuinely confusing right now what a career even looks like. My read is we’ll see many more small creative companies, and more room for people who want to go do their own thing right out of school. Organizations like Pentagram are always going to exist too. It’s still a fairly mentorship-driven field.”

One last question was what he’d say directly to design educators, and his answer is the one we keep coming back to. “There are two narratives out there,” he said. “One says AI is terrible, and there are real concerns worth taking seriously, but that narrative usually misses the creative possibilities. The other says we don’t need creatives anymore, that taste doesn’t matter. I disagree. A model is trained on what people have already made. Somebody still has to piece things together. What design fundamentally is matters more than ever.”

Matt Owens

Co-Founder and Chief Design and Innovation Officer, Athletics

Full excerpt: Interviews/Matt_Owens_Excerpt.md | Interviewed July 1, 2026

Matt Owens is Co-Founder and Chief Design and Innovation Officer at Athletics, a brand studio in Brooklyn, which he started in 2004 that has gone on to build work for Google, Stripe, IBM, and Major League Soccer. He teaches at SVA, holds an MFA from Cranbrook Academy of Art, and spent the late 1990s as art director at the digital agency Methodfive. Long before any of that, he spent over a decade self-publishing an experimental design quarterly, Volumeone, now part of SFMOMA’s permanent collection. Design has been his whole life, not just his job, and that’s exactly why we wanted to talk to him: he’s running a working studio and teaching graduate students at the same time, and his answers move constantly between those two worlds.

What They’re Seeing

Our opening question was how AI is transforming the design process, and he offered a more measured take than we expected. “It’s not transforming the process fundamentally yet,” he said. “AI tools, the new Figma tools, ChatGPT, they’re informing the design process, supplementing it, augmenting it. They haven’t yet transformed the way creative decisions are made.” He gave us a concrete example from his own studio: hiring a 3D artist to build a suite of characters, training a model on that work, then producing more of it faster, while still paying the artist because you need the rights to train on it in the first place. He predicted something bigger coming, though. “In the next year or two, I think you’ll see whole studios built entirely around AI tools, no Adobe products at all. New players, people straight out of school with a design-engineering hybrid skill set, building their own stack from scratch.”

Inside his own studio, he pointed to the work Athletics did with Figma on their keynote presentation, digging into new motion and shader tools that “weren’t ready for prime time” when they started. “I think AI-assisted workflows inside Figma are going to fundamentally change how we deliver brand worlds,” he said. Motion work that used to happen entirely in After Effects, he told us, is starting to move natively into Figma itself. How far that goes, he wasn’t ready to say.

What They’re Hiring For

What tells him a designer understands how to direct AI rather than just accept what it gives them? He said AI barely comes up directly in hiring, but it’s always in the room. “If someone doesn’t have strong foundational skills, typography, form, if their portfolio isn’t at the level we work at, they’re not getting hired,” he said. “None of the designers we interview are using AI out of the box.” He told me about an intern from his SVA class who’d used AI to generate imaginary products inside her own brand world, but the work read as hers because she’d applied real design thinking on top of it. “It’s not about whether you use AI,” he said. “It’s integration versus just accepting whatever comes out of the box.” He was almost proud of his students on this point. “If you’re a student just typing a prompt into Midjourney and dropping it on a page, most of our students are smarter than that. They want to push it further.”

What They Wish Schools Were Teaching

This is where he got the most direct with us. “Fifteen-week class structures are built in a way that doesn’t match real working practice,” he said. “I’ve seen students spend fifteen weeks on something my team turns around in a week or two.” He told me about critiquing an undergraduate program where a teacher had required students to show their entire process on their portfolio site. “Nobody does that,” he said. “A creative director has thirty seconds to look at a portfolio, not thirty minutes. That teacher had students doing something that actively hurts their chances of getting hired.” His conclusion was blunt: if a program doesn’t evolve at the pace of real practice, it stops bringing value to the students inside it.

The Human Layer

How does he tell apart a designer who’s curating and orchestrating AI-generated work from one who’s just shipping whatever it produces? “It comes down to how savvy you are,” he said. “If you’re a genuinely good designer, you can spot bad typography or AI slop with the same instinct.” He described a spectrum: unacceptable, then acceptable and working within the design, and then, at the top, the point where you genuinely can’t tell, where it just feels native. He brought up the Coca-Cola AI-generated ad that made headlines as an example of work that reads as identifiably AI-made, and because of that, carries less cultural weight. “What we’re really evaluating is the level of believability someone’s achieved,” he said. “That’s a cultural and aesthetic muscle, the same one you use to tell when typography isn’t working. You have to train it.”

What Gives Them Hope / The Future

On who’ll matter most in 2027, he described what he calls full-stack creatives: designers who know typography, can build things, and move fluidly between tools. He pointed to his own senior designer, who built tools inside Figma, presented at Config, manages junior designers, and still designs and animates herself. “That doesn’t mean specialists stop bringing value,” he said, “but there’s real risk. Some of our creative directors, around forty, never learned motion, and now they lean on someone younger for that part of the work.” His read on who wins from here: the people who stay curious and keep learning, not the ones who wait around or dismiss what’s new.

His advice for graduating students carried the same thread. At SVA, he ran students through a compressed program built around something closer to operational reality: start with a big idea, then actually touch every beat, motion, type, the system, the story. “Very few students see their work as presenting a world that exists, or could exist,” he told us. “That’s what the best design and brand firms do right now. They build a world using every tool available.” And when we asked what he’d say directly to educators, he didn’t hesitate. “It’s not about the institution. It’s about the students and preparing them for what’s next. You need to be able to keep pace with the people you’re training, the way a coach plays alongside the athletes. The students who make it are the self-initiating ones, but that doesn’t let educators off the hook. You have to push yourself at least as hard as you’re pushing them.”

Julio B. Soler

Creative Director & Senior Creative Producer, Generative AI Film and Video

Julio B. Soler is an award-winning Creative Director and Senior Creative Producer specializing in generative AI film and video production, with campaigns for Verizon, Intel, Merck, NBCUniversal, and AT&T that have earned honors including an Effie Award. He’s a Creator Partner with Higgsfield.ai, CapCut, Dreamina, Flora, and Imagine.Art, and has guest lectured at FIT, San Jose State, and Adobe’s AI For Good conference. He works inside brand campaigns every day, often for brands that are genuinely nervous about AI, so his read on all of this comes from real client risk, not theory.

What They’re Seeing

Julio didn’t hesitate on how AI is transforming the design process. “AI is transforming everything we do in creative fields, there’s no question about it,” he said. “The last three years have been an adventure discovering these tools, but the last six months have been pivotal in how fast adoption has accelerated. I’ve noticed a real shift in attitude among my own students at FIT over that stretch.” He pointed to two specific shifts. “The decision layer has moved way earlier. We’re moving toward an agentic world where you set up a workflow ahead of time and review what it produces, so you’re deciding which tools to chain together before you even start. That makes you far more strategic if you actually know these tools well. And workflows are becoming extremely personalized. Back in 2024, everyone in AI video was chasing the same Runway and Midjourney workflows. Now everyone has their own combination.”

The other shift he named was speed. “In the past, a designer would work through two or three ideas and discern the better one. Now you can do that in minutes, and instead of two ideas, you’re generating ten,” he said. That, he told us, raises the bar for what his students will be expected to do the moment they enter the market. It also raises the stakes on judgment. “The role of the creative director becomes crucial. You need someone with the taste to discern between not two or three good ideas, but hundreds of them. That’s what I’d tell your students: the ability to select from hundreds of strong options is how they show they’re amplifying their own taste with AI, instead of letting the tools replace it.”

What They’re Hiring For

Given how much of his own work runs through generative video, we wanted to know what tells him a candidate actually understands how to direct AI. “What tells me is whether they understand the medium,” he said. “They’re not using AI for AI’s sake, and when they chain tools together, they’re doing it with purpose. If I’m hiring an AI filmmaker, I want them to know basic things: when do you use Kling versus Seedance, when do you use Veo versus something else.” He illustrated this with a recent World Cup campaign for a client that was genuinely cautious about using AI. “We needed very specific soccer physics, and if you didn’t know that Kling is the one tool that actually gets soccer physics right, you couldn’t have pulled off that campaign. If your team only had Veo, you’d have spent hours trying to tame it and never gotten there.”

He also wants to see people push a model past what it was built for. “I’ve seen this in your students at the hackathons: they understand a specific model well enough to push it somewhere I wouldn’t have thought of myself. Somebody figured out how to generate a flash-frame of one image to produce multiple camera angles of a single scene, then export all of them at once. I never would have thought of that.” Underneath all of it, he still wants fundamentals. “You need real grounding in aesthetics, art history, and visual storytelling. That has to exist whether or not you use AI. Without it, you’re just generating.” And the clearest signal of all, he said, is whether someone can explain their process: why these tools, why this order, why this workflow. That’s what tells him someone knows what they’re doing.

What They Wish Schools Were Teaching

When we asked what he’d say directly to design educators, his answer was immediate. “Bet on AI. We’re well past the point where people were debating whether AI belonged in creative fields. These technologies are here to stay, at both the professional and educational levels.” Then he added the caveat that mattered more to me. “You can’t replace the fundamentals with AI. AI only amplifies the creative instinct and knowledge that’s already there. So bet on AI, but don’t forget the fundamentals. Make sure your students hold onto the things that got us here.” He mentioned, almost in passing, that we might be watching the early end of software as we’ve known it, tools like Claude Code raising the question of why anyone keeps paying for Photoshop or Figma when they could potentially build their own version. Things, he said, are getting genuinely fluid.

The Human Layer

Julio’s answer to how he tells apart a designer who’s curating and orchestrating AI work from one who’s just shipping whatever comes out doubled as a small creative-direction masterclass. “You need criteria. You need fundamentals. You have to know your stuff first, and then AI becomes something that amplifies that taste, not something that replaces the need for it,” he said. He mentioned a rule he uses in his own practice: “In creative direction, we use a ten percent rule. Of everything your team generates, only about ten percent should move forward. Be discriminating. Only keep what you truly believe in, even though that’s hard when you’ve fallen in love with your own work.” And the ability to explain your workflow, to a client or your own team, why this tool, in what order, for what purpose, is what separates real direction from noise.

He was direct about how visible the difference actually is. “You can tell, independent of anyone’s tools or AI knowledge, whether someone comes from a solid, formative background in design or filmmaking versus someone who arrived purely through prompting. It’s a huge difference, and you can spot it from a mile away. It’s taste and criteria versus the lack of it.” Then he told us a story that’s stayed with us since: the running joke among his more cautious clients is the fear of “AI slop,” and he uses one example to draw the line every time. “People bring up what McDonald’s did last Christmas constantly. What they forget is that Coca-Cola ran a major AI Christmas campaign the same season, and nobody complained, because you couldn’t tell the difference between their AI animation and traditional CGI. McDonald’s tried to make something cute and funny, and what came out was slop, a bad script, a weak idea, and AI thrown at it to try to rescue something that was never going to work. The backlash wasn’t about AI. It was about a bad idea and a bad execution. It’s not the tools, it’s how you use them. A good idea and solid execution will win the day every time, even if you drew it on a napkin.”

What Gives Them Hope / The Future

The story he told with the most warmth was about a brand campaign that drew from the company’s own library of real contract workers, people who had volunteered their photos for campaigns, and transformed them into elite soccer players for the World Cup. “You’d see a real cleaning professional doing a perfect Messi-style pirouette in the air. That whole thing cost less than a thousand dollars in credits and my own time, and suddenly, we had assets for every social channel. People’s jaws dropped.” That’s the future he’s most excited about: AI as a multiplier for things that were never affordable before. “Even six months ago, an industry like Hollywood would have crucified you for mentioning AI on a project. Now it’s being used on shows that are already on the air. I think it’s important for your students to see these real scenarios happening in the world right now, not just theory.”

Kathleen O’Brien

Advisory Visual Designer, CIO Design, IBM

Kathleen O’Brien is a product designer focused on creating experiences that balance business impact with human needs. In IBM’s CIO organization, she designs and delivers AI-powered solutions that help employees work more efficiently, make better decisions, and have more satisfying workplace experiences. She thrives at the intersection of strategy, design, and technology, partnering closely with stakeholders, product teams, and developers to turn complex challenges into practical, user-centered solutions. Passionate about emerging technologies, Kathleen is particularly interested in how AI and automation can simplify work, unlock creativity, and drive meaningful outcomes for both employees and organizations. We wanted her perspective because she sits on both sides of this shift at once: she designs AI-powered tools for IBM’s own employees, and she uses those same kinds of tools, sanctioned and expected by her own company, in her daily practice.

What They’re Seeing

Kathleen’s read on how AI is transforming the design process came with real ambivalence. “I have mixed feelings about it, because in general designers are expected to do more and faster, and I don’t totally believe that’s because we should be. I think AI is being used as a reason to say that we should be,” she said. She doesn’t think the acceleration is fully here yet, since most companies are still in an experimental phase, but she’s certain something is already transforming: speed itself. “If everything’s moving faster, it’s a lot easier to lose track of what the actual problem is you’re trying to solve. When anyone can create almost anything, what’s actually worth making?” That’s part of why she thinks designers matter more now, not less: judgment and communication become the differentiators, and she’s watched her own role turn increasingly into keeping people focused on the real problem, reacting to AI-generated output rather than producing it herself.

On what the workflow actually looks like inside IBM, she was candid that the maturity many companies advertise isn’t quite real yet. “There’s definitely not a standard,” she said. Designers there are expected to use a tool called IBM Bob, which she described, off the record, as essentially a chat window inside an IDE that lets designers build coded prototypes and work directly with code. Some hold back, worried they’ll break something, and she thinks that hesitation traces back to a gap in education around the tools rather than a lack of will. But she’s watched the culture shift regardless. “I’m seeing designers not just copy code from GitHub anymore, but actually contribute pull requests, and share whatever code or idea they generated directly with developers,” she said, instead of just recording a screen capture of an isolated prototype. “It’s still the Wild West. It’s expected that we use the tools. It’s just not regimented how we work with them.”

What They’re Hiring For

Kathleen was upfront that she doesn’t have hiring experience yet, so we asked instead what she’d expect a junior designer joining her team to already know. Her answer circled back to a signal she trusts more than tool fluency: whether someone changes anything at all. “It’s very clear when a designer uses AI and changes nothing,” she said. She pointed to a website that lets you compare how different models respond to the identical prompt, generating a UI from each one. “The most impactful designers are the ones who actually care about design and won’t take that output at face value. Even the tools that are getting pretty good, the purple gradients, the same card layout, still don’t have a voice of their own. Eventually everything just starts to look like one thing if you let it. So the question is: how do you take it a step further?”

What They Wish Schools Were Teaching

Kathleen graduated seven years ago, and she was candid that even she doesn’t have a firm grasp on how much AI will augment her own job in the near term, which she thinks makes it genuinely hard to say what schools should be teaching right now. “In my day-to-day, it feels like I’m exceeding the expectations of my written role, but there’s more wanted,” she said. “You’re actually expected to code now. You’re expected to be more transformative. The bar keeps changing.”

She used to love the nitty-gritty of the craft, perfecting each screen, accounting for every edge case, though she noted software absorbed a lot of that work already. What she’d want to see more of is a generalist who can orchestrate AI rather than someone who only works screen by screen, and she thinks that starts with a real understanding of design systems built so they can actually be used by generative AI. “We have a really good MCP for our design system, and that empowers me to do a lot,” she said. “Other teams that don’t have design systems as mature as ours don’t have that same leverage.” Content strategy and brand and visual language sit inside that same skill set, she said, and she’d add one more thing she wishes she knew more about herself: micro-interactions and motion, which she thinks will be one of the things that sets interfaces apart once everyone is generating with the same tools.

The Human Layer

On telling apart a designer who’s curating AI work from one who’s just shipping it, Kathleen’s answer was almost counterintuitive. “If a designer is using AI thoughtfully, you wouldn’t know just from looking at their work,” she said. There’s a list, she estimated fifteen to twenty items long, of tells you can spot in an AI-generated UI: layout choices, copy choices, and the like. She’s also noticed a wave of what she called hype pieces, portfolios with beautiful animations where the actual design gets overshadowed. “You made a great-looking interface, but I can’t find the thing I’m actually looking for on it,” she said. The designers using AI well, in her experience, are hard to spot precisely because they’re not shipping flashier work, they’re just working faster and spending the time they free up experiencing culture and learning more. “I haven’t found that the final deliverables from AI are good enough yet, personally,” she said. “It shows up deeper in the process than in the final deliverable.”

That belief extends to how she sees her own role. She thinks she’s respected more for how she navigates stakeholders and tough corporate conversations than for any single deliverable, and she expects that to become true of the profession more broadly. “I’m hearing from product owners at other companies whose expectation is that they’re delivering vibe-coded concept cars,” she said. “We need to be able to react to that, play nice with it, and push back in a way that gets to the root of what they’re actually trying to communicate. I think egos have to drop a bit, overall.”

What Gives Them Hope / The Future

Kathleen described riding a roller coaster between “everything’s amazing” and “we’re doomed,” and her honest read is that it will even out. What she’s confident about is that this has stopped being a job you can do without caring. “I’m honestly shocked by how much I learn from X, more than LinkedIn,” she said. “That’s where I learned about Claude, about having a Claude account. You need to put in effort well beyond going to work to stay attuned to these tools.” Her advice to a student graduating today was to lean into exactly that curiosity, since so many people already in the industry have stopped being genuinely interested. “Designers have always had that curiosity, and that’s why we’ll continue to thrive,” she said.

She was careful to balance that against ego. “There are designers out there who will blindly execute whatever a product owner asks for, and I think that’s totally going to be gone,” she said. “There’s a right way to push back and set constraints, because if all they wanted was a pretty screen, they could get that without us now.” She named motion as a skill on the rise, and real users as a constant she doesn’t want the field to lose. “You can go to AI, get a design, get a critique, and it’s all nice, but it can be a little sad, honestly,” she said. “It’s nice to be able to go back to real humans and keep that innately human piece of the job.” Asked what she’d say to design educators, she didn’t hesitate. “It’s hard for me to say much, because you and CJ do a fabulous job. If you two hadn’t tried, this kind of education wouldn’t have moved anywhere. I felt very prepared when I graduated. You’re out there fighting the good fight.”

Jenna Cao is the founder of Vibe Code Mama, a platform exploring how AI is reshaping work, creativity, and modern life through education, community, and real-world experimentation, and before that, she spent more than fifteen years in strategy and go-to-market roles at ByteDance and across TikTok, CapCut, Dreamina, Shutterstock, L’Oréal, and Citi. She isn’t a designer. She’s a strategist who has spent her whole career standing right next to design, close enough to feel its pull, and we loved that about her. We wanted to know what orchestrating AI looks like from that vantage point, from someone building an entire company with it, not just a single campaign.

What They’re Seeing

The question we ask everyone is what’s the biggest shift AI has brought to the creative process. Jenna didn’t pause. “The biggest shift is that design has become conversational and iterative,” she said. “Designers can now explore dozens of directions in minutes instead of committing to one concept deeply before getting feedback. That’s a real freedom, an expressive way of thinking things through. A designer’s role changes from producing artifacts to directing systems. More experienced designers know what to ask, what to keep, and what to discard faster and more instinctively. It’s really about compressing time and increasing the importance of taste and contextual decision-making. In many ways, AI is shifting value away from execution alone and toward judgment. As creating becomes easier, knowing what to build, why it matters, and when it’s ready becomes the real differentiator.”

We loved how much that echoed what we hear from our own students, so we leaned in and asked her to go a layer deeper: what does her own AI-native workflow actually look like day to day, running a founder’s brand rather than a design studio? She answered like someone who’d thought about this a lot. “Everything is integrated throughout the workflow,” she said. “There’s a human at the center, and everything else, AI included, plays a supporting role. I’m researching audiences and trends, brainstorming concepts collaboratively, looking at different drafts of visuals and structures: faster iteration across the board. But refinement is human judgment. When the work feels intentional and right, that’s when I step in to orchestrate and direct.”

What They’re Hiring For

Given her years in strategy, we wanted to know what tells her whether someone can really direct AI, or whether they’re just accepting whatever it hands them. She didn’t hesitate. “The signal is how they articulate trade-offs: why this output over that one,” she said. “That’s the human judgment, the critical eye. If you just accept the first iteration, it’s hard to say you have judgment built in. In my experience, the first iteration is never the right one. If you have a high bar, there are always tweaks you want to make, and the real challenge is knowing when to stop. It’s somewhere in the middle.”

Next, we turned to the skill that’s fading and the one that’s becoming rare. Her answer surprised me a little, coming from someone with her background. “Craft and execution matter, but they’re becoming less differentiated,” she told me. “The real skill is how quickly you’re motivated to keep experimenting, and how well you synthesize across disciplines: a business mindset when you’re doing design, a social-media mindset when you’re looking at a website. It’s a trans-media world. Systems thinking and interdisciplinary learning, connecting what the founder is saying, what the designer is saying, and what the user is saying, is a new kind of entrepreneurial skill. It’s valuable, and it’s rare.”

What They Wish Schools Were Teaching

She was quick to admit she never went through design school herself, and that honesty made what came next land even more. “I didn’t go to design school myself, so this is an outsider’s view,” she said. “But the next generation obviously needs to learn AI-native workflows. I still think human-centered design as a philosophy, and the emotional intelligence that comes with it, will stay important alongside machine capabilities. Those are good foundations to build on. I’d also encourage students to spend more time collaborating across disciplines. Increasingly, the best work happens when designers, engineers, marketers, product managers, and founders learn to think together rather than in silos.” We appreciated how grounded that answer was. She wasn’t performing with expertise. She simply told me what she’d want if she were the one hiring.

The Human Layer

How does she tell apart a designer who’s genuinely curating AI work from one who’s just shipping whatever comes out? That was our next question, and she thought about it for a moment before answering. “Intentionality is the tell, though it doesn’t show up just one way,” she said. “Can they see the whole stack of decisions, the business rationale, the go-to-market, the audience resonance, what success looks like, and translate that into brand voice? That takes judgment.” Intentionality. That word kept surfacing, interview after interview in this chapter, from designers and non-designers alike, as if everyone had quietly arrived at the same truth from different directions.

What Gives Them Hope / The Future

We ended, the way we always do, by asking what advice she’d give a design student today. Her answer was warm and direct. “Don’t wait for permission,” she said. “Just go make things: make TikToks, build a landing page, try to drive people to it. The most beautiful design doesn’t matter if no one’s coming to it. Put things out there and build in public. Young students have an advantage: curiosity, fewer responsibilities, and more energy to lean into it.”

Then, almost as an afterthought, she added something we’ve since repeated to our own students more than once. “Treat AI as part of the creative process, but understand its fundamentals before you try to direct it: what machine learning is, what generative AI is, what an LLM is, what diffusion models are. That foundational understanding gives you the confidence to actually improve on it. Some things you can’t hack your way through. You have to be patient and let lived experience and trial and error do their work over time.”

Closing

Ten conversations, ten different corners of the industry: a founder, a tool builder, a studio principal, a creative director, an education lead, a filmmaker building the tools other designers are just discovering, a UX design lead who shapes how a model behaves, a designer building AI tools for a global enterprise’s own workforce, a chief creative officer, and a head of design who also chairs a graduate program. We didn’t go looking for consensus, and we didn’t get uniform answers. What we got instead was something more useful: the same handful of convictions, arrived at independently, by people who have never met each other. Taste. Judgment. Integration over acceptance. The ability to explain a decision. That is the vocabulary of the Five Pillars, spoken by people who have never read this book.

We started this chapter expecting to find a gap between what industry wants and what schools teach. What we actually found was narrower, and more encouraging, than that. The people building the tools, running the studios, and shipping the campaigns are not asking for graduates who know more software. They’re asking for exactly what the Five Pillars were built to produce.
