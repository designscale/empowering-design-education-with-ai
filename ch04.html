Chapter 4: The Creative Industry

“AI will not replace designers; designers who use AI will replace designers who don’t.” C.J. Yeh

Every chapter so far has made its case from inside the classroom. This one steps outside it.

Over the past several weeks I sat down with people who are not academics: a founder who built a company on AI-native workflows after fifteen years in strategy at ByteDance, the CEO of a generative tool used by hundreds of creative teams, a studio principal who has been running a brand practice in Brooklyn since 2004, a creative director whose entire specialty is generative film for brands too cautious to say the word “AI” out loud a year ago, a product designer who runs AI education for five hundred designers at Meta, and a chief creative officer building campaigns for Google and Peloton out of studios in Sydney, Los Angeles, and New York. I asked each of them some version of the same five questions: what’s actually changing, what they’re hiring for, what they wish design schools were teaching, where the human contribution still lives, and what gives them hope.

What follows are those conversations, presented one at a time, in each person’s own words. Two more, with Randy Hunt and Eunju Kim, are still in progress as I write this; I’ve marked those below and will fill them in once they’re complete.

Karen Lin

Product Designer, Meta

Full excerpt: Interviews/Karen_Lin_Excerpt.md | Interviewed July 2, 2026

Karen Lin is a Product Designer at Meta, where she designs social experiences for 3.4 billion people across Facebook, Instagram, and WhatsApp, and leads AI education for a team of more than 500 designers. Before Meta, she built design systems at Atlassian, and before that, she was an architect who designed skyscrapers. She grew up in Australia and now calls New York home. Karen runs AI education for one of the largest design organizations in the industry, so her answers carry the weight of watching hundreds of designers adopt these tools in real time, not just her own workflow.

What They’re Seeing

Karen’s read on how AI is transforming the design process started with a culture shift she’s watched unfold inside Meta itself. “Prototyping culture is a lot stronger now,” she said. “Before, we lived in Figma and used a tool called Origami, but it was hard to learn and a lot of people just didn’t use it. Now that prototyping is so easy with vibe coding, Cursor, VS Code, Claude, sometimes Figma Make, so much more of the organization is empowered to build and share ideas. It used to be just designers building and sharing. Now some of our design managers will have an idea and vibe-code it in ten minutes. Even PMs do it. The design process doesn’t just belong to designers anymore.”

The other shift she named was about fidelity. “I used to polish my designs for ages before showing anyone,” she said. “Now I’ll quickly vibe-code a rough sketch and show it to my cross-functional partners early, and only after we’re aligned do I move to high fidelity, instead of trying to build the perfect thing on the first try.”

What They’re Hiring For

Karen’s hiring bar has less to do with whether someone uses AI and more to do with whether they check it. “There’s always a quality bar I check,” she said. “AI isn’t perfect, it hallucinates, and I definitely notice when someone doesn’t check their work after it spits something out. If someone understands how to direct AI, their work still uses it, but it’s high quality, and they’re often working at a higher volume because of it.” She pays close attention to how people prompt. “A weak prompt is, here’s a problem, give me one answer. A good designer prompts something more like: give me three options, critique yourself, weigh the pros and cons, then recommend the best one and explain why. That’s a completely different level of thinking.”

She leads Meta’s design education team of five hundred people, and her own immediate team of seven designers now ships code directly into production instead of staying in Figma. “If I were hiring a designer today, I’d love to hire someone comfortable shipping code,” she said. “That doesn’t mean they need to be great at it immediately, but they need to show proactivity, curiosity, and a willingness to try new tools. A lot of us on my team only learned to code a few months ago ourselves.”

What They Wish Schools Were Teaching

This is where Karen got the most animated. “I have strong opinions about this. Design school should teach people how to have good taste, because not all designers have it,” she said. “AI can spit out something generic, but a designer with good taste will pick the version everyone looks at and says, that’s beautifully designed. In a world where a machine can generate infinite iterations, the most valuable designers are the ones who can curate with real taste.” She’s genuinely wrestled with whether taste can be trained into someone who wasn’t born with it. “I’ll probably always be bad at soccer, no matter how much I train. But taste, I think, is more fake-it-till-you-make-it. When I’m designing something I don’t know much about, I go get inspired, a gallery, Pinterest, people who objectively have good taste, and I notice what they choose. By imitating people with good taste, I start to develop my own.”

She lit up describing a student night of Christie’s that had genuinely surprised her. “I’ve been in corporate design for six or seven years, working on very serious products, growth, monetization, enterprise tools. There’s no room to be playful there. But so many of the student portfolios I saw were genuinely fun. I’d land on one and think, this is like a toy, I just want to click around and explore. I think a lot of us who’ve been working a while lose touch with that creativity. The students still have it.”

The Human Layer

On telling apart a designer who’s curating and orchestrating AI work from one who’s just shipping whatever it produces, Karen said the tell is the same one she uses in hiring. “If you’re curating and orchestrating, the work is higher quality, and you’ll probably deliver more of it. If someone is just shipping whatever it produces, I catch it right away: small mistakes, hallucinations, things that clearly weren’t thoughtfully put together.”

What Gives Them Hope / The Future

Karen’s picture of who matters most in 2027 kept circling back to curiosity. “You don’t have to be great at vibe coding, but you have to be willing to pick up new tools as they come out. There are very good designers who, when something new shows up, say they’ll just stick with Figma because they’re better in it. The ones who stay open are the ones who’ll succeed.” She brought up something Jensen Huang said that stuck with her: the people who matter most are the ones who can see around corners and predict what’s coming. “Nobody can actually predict where this goes, but the people who develop a feel for where things are heading are the ones who’ll be valuable.”

Her advice for a student building a career in a field moving this fast was refreshingly practical. “Learn to prompt well, because no matter what tool you’re using, you’ll need that skill,” she said. She’d spent real energy reassuring her own team that the pace wasn’t as overwhelming as it felt. “Most of these tools group into similar buckets. If you learn to vibe-code in Cursor, you can pick up Claude or VS Code just as fast. Learn one tool from each bucket and fifty more can come out, you’ll pick each one up in no time.” And when I asked what she’d say to design educators, her answer surprised me by being about people, not tools. “Teach the human touch: not just when to use AI, but when not to. We use AI constantly at Meta, but if you’re trying to build alignment with a stakeholder, an AI-drafted message isn’t always the right move. Being human and authentic is what actually gets your designs shipped. Not because a bot talked to them. Because they like you as a person.”

Weber Wong

Founder & CEO, FLORA

Full excerpt: Interviews/Weber_Wong_Excerpt.md | Interviewed July 1, 2026

Weber Wong is the Founder & CEO of FLORA, a professional creative tool that brings the best generative models together onto one infinite canvas. Before founding it, he was a graduate student at NYU’s ITP, a master’s program focused on creative technology. Talking with Weber felt different from the other interviews in this chapter. He isn’t just using generative tools, he built one, and he’s watched hundreds of creative teams adopt it in real time. I wanted his read on what’s actually changing, from someone standing at the center of the shift rather than downstream of it.

What They’re Seeing

My first question for him was how AI is fundamentally transforming the design process, not just the tools but the way creative decisions get made. He didn’t answer with a hot take, he answered with history. “Adobe was founded in the 1980s to build creative interfaces for the personal computing paradigm: controlling the graphical user interface, controlling individual pixels, to make one piece of media at a time,” he said. “Generative tools are fundamentally different. It’s top down. You ask for what you want, you get a rough eighty percent version, and then you chisel away from there to get exactly what you want. In some ways that’s actually more natural. It’s more like carving: you move away the big chunks, then chisel toward the fine details.”

I wanted to know what that looks like in practice, inside his own studio. He walked me through the whole arc of a creative project: an idea, divergent exploration, a convergent decision, then production and refinement. “Traditionally, that first exploration step is enormously time intensive,” he told me. “One of our first customers, a designer at Pentagram, used to get asked to explore hundreds of variations of a typeface by hand, in Illustrator. It would take days. Now she can generate hundreds of variants, choose the one that’s conceptually right with her creative director, and refine from there.” He lit up describing how that process can now be codified into something repeatable, an entire workflow that reflects a person’s own creative judgment, reusable with infinite variation. “It lets creatives go from zero to one much faster, and once they know what they want, from one to a thousand,” he said. “I think generative tools will make the world more beautiful, because it takes less creative labor to get an idea into the world, and that means more people can drive their own ideas forward instead of just executing someone else’s.”

What They’re Hiring For

What tells him a designer understands how to direct AI, rather than just accepting whatever it hands them? I put that to him directly. “What’s valuable today is taste, judgment, direction, the ability to look at a creative problem and piece together visual, cultural, and other references into an effective solution,” he said. Execution, he added, is a different story. “I do think the value of creative execution is going down. Across the industry, I’d say eighty to ninety percent of creative time goes to executing an idea rather than figuring out what it should be. In ten or twenty years, people will look back at how much time we spent learning what one button does versus another and be amazed.”

He compared it to disruptions he’d seen before, horses giving way to cars, and offered a smaller, more personal one: he throws pottery, and most people now own mass-produced plates that are genuinely good, even as there are fewer village potters left. “I think the shift is from taste-plus-execution to taste-plus-the-ability-to-build-creative-systems,” he said. He was careful to note that knowing the tools well isn’t the same as having judgment. “There’s an emerging class of AI-forward agencies who mostly get paid for a temporary information advantage, not for deeper creative direction. I think everyone catches up to the tools eventually. What has always mattered is having great ideas and being able to execute them well as a team.”

What They Wish Schools Were Teaching

Weber didn’t hesitate when I asked what he thought design schools should be doing differently. “I think what you two are doing is pretty spot on,” he said. “Traditional education is still necessary, if not more so. I took a class at ITP called Visual Language, and if I hadn’t taken it, I wouldn’t know what good composition even looks like.” What changes, he said, is the tool underneath the thinking, not the thinking itself. “When we taught style transfer in Flora, to do it well you actually have to articulate what you want in a way that’s closer to how you’d think in a visual language class, not further from it.”

The Human Layer

How does he tell apart a designer who’s genuinely curating AI work from one who’s just shipping whatever it produces? “You can tell,” he said simply. “I don’t think a piece of work is inherently better because someone added a Photoshop layer, and I don’t think it’s inherently worse because it was AI-generated. I don’t think AI is a new medium, exactly. It’s a new method. What humans find compelling stays the same.” Then he told me something one of his own customers keeps asking him: can Flora build a model to judge whether something is on brand? His answer says everything about where he thinks the real work still lives. “I always think, isn’t that your job? What does on-brand even mean? That’s the point of having a person there.”

What Gives Them Hope / The Future

For the last stretch, I wanted his take on what kind of designer will matter most in 2027, and what he’d tell a student graduating today. “The designers who matter most are the ones with taste, plus AI literacy, plus enough curiosity and ambition to keep learning in a moment that’s changing this fast,” he said. He was honest about the uncertainty in that answer too. “It’s genuinely confusing right now what a career even looks like. My read is we’ll see many more small creative companies, and more room for people who want to go do their own thing right out of school. Organizations like Pentagram are always going to exist too. It’s still a fairly mentorship-driven field.”

One last question was what he’d say directly to design educators, and his answer is the one I keep coming back to. “There are two narratives out there,” he said. “One says AI is terrible, and there are real concerns worth taking seriously, but that narrative usually misses the creative possibilities. The other says we don’t need creatives anymore, that taste doesn’t matter. I disagree. A model is trained on what people have already made. Somebody still has to piece things together. What design fundamentally is matters more than ever.”

Matt Owens

Co-Founder and Chief Design and Innovation Officer, Athletics

Full excerpt: Interviews/Matt_Owens_Excerpt.md | Interviewed July 1, 2026

Matt Owens is Co-Founder and Chief Design and Innovation Officer at Athletics, a brand studio in Brooklyn he started in 2004 that has gone on to build work for Google, Stripe, IBM, and Major League Soccer. He teaches at SVA, holds an MFA from Cranbrook Academy of Art, and spent the late 1990s as art director at the digital agency Methodfive. Long before any of that, he spent over a decade self-publishing an experimental design quarterly, Volumeone, now part of SFMOMA’s permanent collection. Design has been his whole life, not just his job, and that’s exactly why I wanted to talk to him: he’s running a working studio and teaching graduate students at the same time, and his answers move constantly between those two worlds.

What They’re Seeing

My opening question was how AI is transforming the design process, and he offered a more measured take than I expected. “It’s not transforming the process fundamentally yet,” he said. “AI tools, the new Figma tools, ChatGPT, they’re informing the design process, supplementing it, augmenting it. They haven’t yet transformed the way creative decisions are made.” He gave me a concrete example from his own studio: hiring a 3D artist to build a suite of characters, training a model on that work, then producing more of it faster, while still paying the artist because you need the rights to train on it in the first place. He predicted something bigger coming, though. “In the next year or two, I think you’ll see whole studios built entirely around AI tools, no Adobe products at all. New players, people straight out of school with a design-engineering hybrid skill set, building their own stack from scratch.”

Inside his own studio, he pointed to the work Athletics did with Figma on their keynote presentation, digging into new motion and shader tools that “weren’t ready for prime time” when they started. “I think AI-assisted workflows inside Figma are going to fundamentally change how we deliver brand worlds,” he said. Motion work that used to happen entirely in After Effects, he told me, is starting to move natively into Figma itself. How far that goes, he wasn’t ready to say.

What They’re Hiring For

What tells him a designer understands how to direct AI rather than just accept what it gives them? He said AI barely comes up directly in hiring, but it’s always in the room. “If someone doesn’t have strong foundational skills, typography, form, if their portfolio isn’t at the level we work at, they’re not getting hired,” he said. “None of the designers we interview are using AI out of the box.” He told me about an intern from his SVA class who’d used AI to generate imaginary products inside her own brand world, but the work read as hers because she’d applied real design thinking on top of it. “It’s not about whether you use AI,” he said. “It’s integration versus just accepting whatever comes out of the box.” He was almost proud of his students on this point. “If you’re a student just typing a prompt into Midjourney and dropping it on a page, most of our students are smarter than that. They want to push it further.”

What They Wish Schools Were Teaching

This is where he got the most direct with me. “Fifteen-week class structures are built in a way that doesn’t match real working practice,” he said. “I’ve seen students spend fifteen weeks on something my team turns around in a week or two.” He told me about critiquing an undergraduate program where a teacher had required students to show their entire process on their portfolio site. “Nobody does that,” he said. “A creative director has thirty seconds to look at a portfolio, not thirty minutes. That teacher had students doing something that actively hurts their chances of getting hired.” His conclusion was blunt: if a program doesn’t evolve at the pace of real practice, it stops bringing value to the students inside it.

The Human Layer

How does he tell apart a designer who’s curating and orchestrating AI-generated work from one who’s just shipping whatever it produces? “It comes down to how savvy you are,” he said. “If you’re a genuinely good designer, you can spot bad typography or AI slop with the same instinct.” He described a spectrum: unacceptable, then acceptable and working within the design, and then, at the top, the point where you genuinely can’t tell, where it just feels native. He brought up the Coca-Cola AI-generated ad that made headlines as an example of work that reads as identifiably AI-made, and because of that, carries less cultural weight. “What we’re really evaluating is the level of believability someone’s achieved,” he said. “That’s a cultural and aesthetic muscle, the same one you use to tell when typography isn’t working. You have to train it.”

What Gives Them Hope / The Future

On who’ll matter most in 2027, he described what he calls full-stack creatives: designers who know typography, can build things, and move fluidly between tools. He pointed to his own senior designer, Sunho, who built tools inside Figma, presented at Config, manages junior designers, and still designs and animates herself. “That doesn’t mean specialists stop bringing value,” he said, “but there’s real risk. Some of our creative directors, around forty, never learned motion, and now they lean on someone younger for that part of the work.” His read on who wins from here: the people who stay curious and keep learning, not the ones who wait around or dismiss what’s new.

His advice for graduating students carried the same thread. At SVA, he ran students through a compressed program built around something closer to operational reality: start with a big idea, then actually touch every beat, motion, type, the system, the story. “Very few students see their work as presenting a world that exists, or could exist,” he told me. “That’s what the best design and brand firms do right now. They build a world using every tool available.” And when I asked what he’d say directly to educators, he didn’t hesitate. “It’s not about the institution. It’s about the students and preparing them for what’s next. You need to be able to keep pace with the people you’re training, the way a coach plays alongside the athletes. The students who make it are the self-initiating ones, but that doesn’t let educators off the hook. You have to push yourself at least as hard as you’re pushing them.”

Julio B. Soler

Creative Director & Senior Creative Producer, Generative AI Film and Video

Full excerpt: Interviews/Julio_Soler_Excerpt.md | Interviewed July 1, 2026

Julio B. Soler is an award-winning Creative Director and Senior Creative Producer specializing in generative AI film and video production, with campaigns for Verizon, Intel, Merck, NBCUniversal, and AT&T that have earned honors including an Effie Award. He’s a Creator Partner with Higgsfield.ai, SkyReels.ai, Electric Sheep, and Imagine.Art, and has guest lectured at FIT, San Jose State, and Adobe’s AI For Good conference. He works inside brand campaigns every day, often for brands that are genuinely nervous about AI, so his read on all of this comes from real client risk, not theory.

What They’re Seeing

Julio didn’t hesitate on how AI is transforming the design process. “AI is transforming everything we do in creative fields, there’s no question about it,” he said. “The last three years have been an adventure discovering these tools, but the last six months have been pivotal in how fast adoption has accelerated. I’ve noticed a real shift in attitude among my own students at FIT over that stretch.” He pointed to two specific shifts. “The decision layer has moved way earlier. We’re moving toward an agentic world where you set up a workflow ahead of time and review what it produces, so you’re deciding which tools to chain together before you even start. That makes you far more strategic if you actually know these tools well. And workflows are becoming extremely personalized. Back in 2024, everyone in AI video was chasing the same Runway and Midjourney workflows. Now everyone has their own combination.”

The other shift he named was speed. “In the past, a designer would work through two or three ideas and discern the better one. Now you can do that in minutes, and instead of two ideas, you’re generating ten,” he said. That, he told me, raises the bar for what his students will be expected to do the moment they enter the market. It also raises the stakes on judgment. “The role of the creative director becomes crucial. You need someone with the taste to discern between not two or three good ideas, but hundreds of them. That’s what I’d tell your students: the ability to select from hundreds of strong options is how they show they’re amplifying their own taste with AI, instead of letting the tools replace it.”

What They’re Hiring For

Given how much of his own work runs through generative video, I wanted to know what tells him a candidate actually understands how to direct AI. “What tells me is whether they understand the medium,” he said. “They’re not using AI for AI’s sake, and when they chain tools together, they’re doing it with purpose. If I’m hiring an AI filmmaker, I want them to know basic things: when do you use Kling versus Seedance, when do you use Veo versus something else.” He offered a vivid example from a Task Rabbit World Cup campaign, a brand that was genuinely cautious about AI. “We needed very specific soccer physics, and if you didn’t know that Kling is the one tool that actually gets soccer physics right, you couldn’t have pulled off that campaign. If your team only had Veo, you’d have spent hours trying to tame it and never gotten there.”

He also wants to see people push a model past what it was built for. “I’ve seen this in your students at the hackathons: they understand a specific model well enough to push it somewhere I wouldn’t have thought of myself. Somebody figured out how to generate a flash-frame of one image to produce multiple camera angles of a single scene, then export all of them at once. I never would have thought of that.” Underneath all of it, he still wants fundamentals. “You need real grounding in aesthetics, art history, visual storytelling. That has to exist whether or not you use AI. Without it, you’re just generating.” And the clearest signal of all, he said, is whether someone can explain their process: why this tool, why this order, why this workflow. That’s what tells him someone knows what they’re doing.

What They Wish Schools Were Teaching

When I asked what he’d say directly to design educators, his answer was immediate. “Bet on AI. We’re well past the point where people were debating whether AI belonged in creative fields. These technologies are here to stay, at both the professional and educational level.” Then he added the caveat that mattered more to me. “You can’t replace the fundamentals with AI. AI only amplifies the creative instinct and knowledge that’s already there. So bet on AI, but don’t forget the fundamentals. Make sure your students hold onto the things that got us here.” He mentioned, almost in passing, that we might be watching the early end of software as we’ve known it, tools like Claude Code raising the question of why anyone keeps paying for Photoshop or Figma when they can build their own version. Things, he said, are getting genuinely fluid.

The Human Layer

Julio’s answer to how he tells apart a designer who’s curating and orchestrating AI work from one who’s just shipping whatever comes out doubled as a small creative-direction masterclass. “You need criteria. You need fundamentals. You have to know your stuff first, and then AI becomes something that amplifies that taste, not something that replaces the need for it,” he said. He mentioned a rule he uses in his own practice: “In creative direction we use a ten percent rule. Of everything your team generates, only about ten percent should move forward. Be discriminating. Only keep what you truly believe in, even though that’s hard when you’ve fallen in love with your own work.” And the ability to explain your workflow, to a client or your own team, why this tool, in what order, for what purpose, is what separates real direction from noise.

He was direct about how visible the difference actually is. “You can tell, independent of anyone’s tools or AI knowledge, whether someone comes from a solid, formative background in design or filmmaking versus someone who arrived purely through prompting. It’s a huge difference, and you can spot it from a mile away. It’s taste and criteria versus the lack of it.” Then he told me a story that’s stayed with me since: the running joke among his more cautious clients is the fear of “AI slop,” and he uses one example to draw the line every time. “People bring up what McDonald’s did last Christmas constantly. What they forget is that Coca-Cola ran a major AI Christmas campaign the same season, and nobody complained, because you couldn’t tell the difference between their AI animation and traditional CGI. McDonald’s tried to make something cute and funny, and what came out was slop, a bad script, a weak idea, AI thrown at it to try to rescue something that was never going to work. The backlash wasn’t about AI. It was about a bad idea and a bad execution. It’s not the tools, it’s how you use them. A good idea and solid execution will win the day every time, even if you drew it on a napkin.”

What Gives Them Hope / The Future

The story he told me with the most warmth was about a Task Rabbit campaign that reused the company’s own library of real taskers, people who volunteer their photos for campaigns, and turned them into elite soccer players for the World Cup. “You’d see a real cleaning professional doing a perfect Messi-style pirouette in the air. That whole thing cost less than a thousand dollars in credits and my time, and suddenly we had assets for every social channel. People’s jaws dropped.” That’s the future he’s most excited about: AI as a multiplier for things that were never affordable before. “Even six months ago, an industry like Hollywood would have crucified you for mentioning AI on a project. Now it’s being used on shows that are already on the air. I think it’s important for your students to see these real scenarios happening in the world right now, not just theory.”

Joey Hunter

Chief Creative Officer & Partner, Entropico

Full excerpt: Interviews/Joey_Hunter_Excerpt.md | Interviewed July 2, 2026

Joey Hunter is Chief Creative Officer and partner at Entropico, a creative agency and production studio with offices in Sydney, Los Angeles, and New York. He started his career as an editor before becoming a director who works with emerging technology like Unreal Engine, and has led campaigns for Google, Peloton, Tripadvisor, and Tidal that have earned him a Triple J Australian Music Video of the Year award and two Webby Awards. Entropico works with large global brands, so his answers are grounded in what actually survives client approval, not just what’s technically possible.

What They’re Seeing

Joey’s read on what an AI-assisted workflow actually looks like inside his studio came with a caveat about scale. “Because we work with big brands who want to say so many different things in different creative ways, what we’ve found most useful is pairing AI with really great practitioners, designers, animators, filmmakers, and using it the way we used to use Google search or YouTube to find reference,” he said. “AI has become a fast track to get our creatives amazing content to work with, almost like clay they can mold. On a Google product video, for example, instead of waiting for real assets, we generate what our ideal assets would be, and that informs the end result. I’d say it’s more like a supercharged content library than anything else for us.”

I asked whether that ever extends to final delivery. “In certain situations, yes,” he said. “It’s usually replacing the kind of work that would’ve been done in 3D or 2D animation anyway, fantasy, larger than life, something surreal. That binary has always existed. There was always a chunk of work you wouldn’t try to do in 3D animation, and it never really threatened the human side of things.” He believes that holds. “There will always be importance placed on the stuff people know was made in real life. Now there’s an additional asset that proves the effort that went into making something real, and that becomes part of the campaign itself. Sometimes a client pays us just as much for the behind-the-scenes as for the campaign.”

What They’re Hiring For

Joey’s answer on directing AI circled back to something more fundamental than tool fluency. “The mindset of a designer, an editor, an animator still has to be applied to whatever they’re doing, for me to know they have the taste and skill to operate at something valuable to us,” he said. “It’s not just taste, it’s the whole range of skills that go into harnessing creativity, applied to whatever tools show up. Vibe coding is a good example right now, because people are bringing their existing art practice into something new with it.” When I pushed on whether he was seeing any skill quietly disappear in younger candidates, he pushed back gently. “I don’t think I’ve actually seen people losing that underlying skill set,” he said. “It’s more a combination of passion and strategic thinking about how you present your skills in an interesting way. A portfolio should have something on it that’s a separate avenue, something a bit surprising, rather than everything being straight down the line.”

What They Wish Schools Were Teaching

Joey had a specific, almost technical answer here. “Design schools should teach one solid 3D program properly, either Blender or Unreal Engine. I’d lean toward Unreal Engine myself, because it opened up my mindset around what can be made artificially versus not, and it was genuinely easy to get into.” He drew a straight line to his own training. “At university in Australia, they taught us the actual history of editing, cutting film strips and pasting them together. I attribute a lot of my own creative process to thinking the way an editor thinks. It’s a lot of time to ask students to invest, and it’s hard to convince them it’s worth it up front, but I really do think it is.”

He lit up describing a piece of student work that had genuinely surprised him. “One of your students, Yuqing, made something like a dream machine, an interactive story with real choose-your-own-adventure elements. That’s the kind of thing that would’ve taken someone a very long time to build in the past, and she did it in a short amount of time while still putting a lot of her own thinking and style into it.”

The Human Layer

On telling apart a designer who’s curating AI work from one who’s just shipping it, Joey’s answer was disarmingly simple. “It’s a combination of how weird something is and how good it is. If something is really good but not weird or risky at all, I think it lacks value these days. If it’s really good and put together in a way that’s a little unexpected, that’s where you can tell a human was actually involved.” He added, “You see more of someone’s personality in the work that way, because if someone’s just shipping whatever comes out, it’s always going to land on the median, the middle version of an anime or a painting, rather than the weird version of it.”

What Gives Them Hope / The Future

Asked who’ll be most valuable in 2027, Joey didn’t reach for a type, he reached for a person. “There’s a small agency run by someone who used to head up motion at YouTube. I’d describe him as a multi-faceted creative strategist with a high technical bar who also deeply understands what a brand needs. He came up with his own agency’s tagline: building brands that know when to misbehave. The fact that he came up with that himself tells you how much strategy sits underneath the design work.” His advice for graduating students followed the same thread. “Investing in slow-build skills might feel like a waste of time, even painful, but it pays off. Learning that way, and figuring out how to apply those skills laterally, is what actually builds a creative-strategy mindset. Otherwise you’re always executing rather than generating ideas that mean something.” And his message to educators doubled as a compliment. “Keep doing what you two are already doing: finding the balance between adopting these new tools and still holding real reverence for the hard creative skills underneath them. That balance is genuinely difficult to hold.”

Jenna Cao

Founder, Vibe Code Mama (Former ByteDance Strategy & GTM Leader)

Full excerpt: Interviews/Jenna_Cao_Excerpt.md | Interviewed July 1, 2026

Jenna Cao is the founder of Vibe Code Mama, a community helping ambitious women build better systems for work and life with AI, and before that she spent more than fifteen years in strategy and go-to-market roles at ByteDance and across TikTok, CapCut, Dreamina, Shutterstock, L’Oréal, and Citi. She isn’t a designer. She’s a strategist who has spent her whole career standing right next to design, close enough to feel its pull, and I loved that about her. I wanted to know what orchestrating AI looks like from that vantage point, from someone building an entire company with it, not just a single campaign.

What They’re Seeing

The question I ask everyone is what’s the biggest shift AI has brought to the creative process. Jenna didn’t pause. “The biggest shift is that design has become conversational and iterative,” she said. “Designers can now explore dozens of directions in minutes instead of committing to one concept deeply before getting feedback. That’s a real freedom, an expressive way of thinking things through. A designer’s role changes from producing artifacts to directing systems. More experienced designers know what to ask, what to keep, what to discard, faster and more instinctively. It’s really about compressing time and increasing the importance of taste and contextual decision-making.”

I loved how much that echoed what I hear from my own students, so I leaned in and asked her to go a layer deeper: what does her own AI-native workflow actually look like day to day, running a founder’s brand rather than a design studio? She answered like someone who’d thought about this a lot. “Everything is integrated throughout the workflow,” she said. “There’s a human at the center, and everything else, AI included, plays a supporting role. I’m researching audiences and trends, brainstorming concepts collaboratively, looking at different drafts of visuals and structures: faster iteration across the board. But refinement is human judgment. When the work feels intentional and right, that’s when I step in to orchestrate and direct.”

What They’re Hiring For

Given her years in strategy, I wanted to know what tells her whether someone can really direct AI, or whether they’re just accepting whatever it hands them. She didn’t hesitate. “The signal is how they articulate trade-offs: why this output over that one,” she said. “That’s the human judgment, the critique eye. If you just accept the first iteration, it’s hard to say you have judgment built in. In my experience, the first iteration is never the right one. If you have a high bar, there are always tweaks you want to make, and the real challenge is knowing when to stop. It’s somewhere in the middle.”

Next I turned to the skill that’s fading, and the one that’s becoming rare. Her answer surprised me a little, coming from someone with her background. “Craft and execution matter, but they’re becoming less differentiated,” she told me. “The real skill is how quickly you’re motivated to keep experimenting, and how well you synthesize across disciplines: a business mindset when you’re doing design, a social-media mindset when you’re looking at a website. It’s a trans-media world. Systems thinking and interdisciplinary learning, connecting what the founder is saying, what the designer is saying, what the user is saying, is a new kind of entrepreneurial skill. It’s valuable, and it’s rare.”

What They Wish Schools Were Teaching

She was quick to admit she never went through design school herself, and that honesty made what came next land even more. “I didn’t go to design school myself, so this is an outsider’s view,” she said. “But the next generation obviously needs to learn AI-native workflows. I still think human-centered design as a philosophy, and the emotional intelligence that comes with it, will stay important alongside machine capabilities. Those are good foundations to build on.” I appreciated how grounded that answer was. She wasn’t performing expertise. She simply told me what she’d want if she were the one hiring.

The Human Layer

How does she tell apart a designer who’s genuinely curating AI work from one who’s just shipping whatever comes out? That was my next question, and she thought about it for a moment before answering. “Intentionality is the tell, though it doesn’t show up just one way,” she said. “Can they see the whole stack of decisions, the business rationale, the go-to-market, the audience resonance, what success looks like, and translate that into brand voice? That takes judgment.” Intentionality. That word kept surfacing, interview after interview in this chapter, from designers and non-designers alike, as if everyone had quietly arrived at the same truth from different directions.

What Gives Them Hope / The Future

I ended, the way I always do, by asking what advice she’d give a design student today. Her answer was warm and direct. “Don’t wait for permission,” she said. “Just go make things: make TikToks, build a landing page, try to drive people to it. The most beautiful design doesn’t matter if no one’s coming to it. Put things out there and build in public. Young students have an advantage: curiosity, fewer responsibilities, and more energy to lean into it.”

Then, almost as an afterthought, she added something I’ve since repeated to my own students more than once. “Treat AI as part of the creative process, but understand its fundamentals before you try to direct it: what machine learning is, what generative AI is, what an LLM is, what diffusion models are. That foundational understanding gives you the confidence to actually improve on it. Some things you can’t hack your way through. You have to be patient and let lived experience and trial and error do their work over time.”

Closing

Six conversations, six different corners of the industry: a founder, a tool builder, a studio principal, a creative director, an education lead, and a chief creative officer. I didn’t go looking for consensus, and I didn’t get uniform answers. What I got instead was something more useful: the same handful of convictions, arrived at independently, by people who have never met each other. Taste. Judgment. Integration over acceptance. The ability to explain a decision. That is the vocabulary of the Five Pillars, spoken by people who have never read this book.

I started this chapter expecting to find a gap between what industry wants and what schools teach. What I actually found was narrower, and more encouraging, than that. The people building the tools, running the studios, and shipping the campaigns are not asking for graduates who know more software. They’re asking for exactly what the Five Pillars were built to produce.

That is the gap this book is trying to close. The next section is the part I can hand to you directly: the framework, adaptable to any program, any discipline, any institution willing to move.
