Chapter 2: The Curriculum Innovation

“AI literacy is less about teaching new tools and more about teaching systemic thinking.” Christie Shin

Building From the Field, Not the Classroom

Most faculty who are talking about AI in design education are doing exactly that: talking about it. Observing it from a careful distance. Reading the research, attending the panels, waiting to see how things settle before committing to a curricular direction.

Our approach has been different.

When I started building this curriculum, I was not theorizing from the sidelines. I was operating in the field, in real time, alongside the same tools and workflows I was bringing into the classroom. At CML, we were integrating AI into live client work: rebranding, digital campaigns, product design. The decisions I was making in studio on Monday were shaping what I taught on Thursday. That feedback loop, between professional practice and classroom instruction, is what makes this curriculum different from most of what exists in design education today.

But the studio work is only part of it. What I’ve built beyond the classroom reflects a deliberate choice to stay inside the field rather than observe it from a faculty position.

In 2021, I became a founding member of the Figma Educator Advisory Board, working directly with Figma’s team on how design tools evolve for the next generation of practitioners. In 2026, I was accepted into the inaugural cohort of the Google Higher Ed Faculty AI Fellowship, one of roughly 65 faculty selected across North America to develop AI-integrated pedagogy at the highest level. That fellowship is not honorary. It is active, rigorous, and culminates in presenting an original Impact Challenge Solution at Google’s campus in Sunnyvale, California.

On the industry partnership side, I’ve built direct educational relationships with the companies defining where AI-Assisted Design is heading. ElevenLabs, whose voice AI tools are reshaping how designers think about sound and brand. Reve and FLORA, two AI startups at the frontier of creative tools, where we’ve built educational programs that give students direct access to tools still being defined. Adobe, where I serve on the AIGA x Adobe Faculty Council and have run the Digital Product Design Challenge since 2019. My relationship with AIGA, the professional association for design, goes back further still: as a long-standing mentor and collaborator, that connection has shaped how I think about what the profession asks of young designers entering it. These are not passive affiliations. They are working relationships, built because I believe that if you want to teach students where the field is going, you have to be in the room where it’s being built.

This is the position from which the curriculum we’re about to describe was designed. Not from a reading list. Not from a conference circuit. From the field itself.

Once we knew the gap was real, once we’d seen it in our studio, felt it in our classroom, and confirmed it in conversation after conversation with hiring managers and creative directors who kept telling us the same thing, the question shifted. It was no longer whether to act. The question was: what do you actually build?

That question is harder than it sounds. The obvious answer is: teach the tools. Add prompting workshops to the intro course. Bring in a generative AI unit. Subscribe to a few platforms, demo them in class, and call it current. A lot of institutions have gone that route. It’s faster. It requires less disruption. And it misses the point almost entirely.

Tools change. In the time it takes to design a curriculum, pilot it, refine it, and run it at scale, the tools the curriculum was built around may already be obsolete. If you teach students to use a specific software, you’ve taught them a perishable skill. The pace of AI development guarantees that. What you need to teach instead is how to think: how to frame a problem, how to move from concept to execution with intelligence and intention, how to direct AI rather than simply operate it.

That insight is what became the Five Pillars of Creative Intelligence.

We arrived at the Five Pillars through a lot of conversation about what we were seeing in professional practice, what we were observing in our students, and what we believed a designer actually needed to be able to do in the age of AI. Not just in entry-level positions, but over the course of a career. Not just with the tools available today, but with whatever comes next.

The first pillar is Problem Framing. It is the most human thing a designer does, and the one AI can least replicate. Before any tool is opened, before any direction is set, a designer has to understand the real problem. Not the stated problem. The human need behind it. This requires empathy, genuine curiosity, and the ability to ask the right questions before jumping to answers. Students who can frame problems clearly and insightfully will always have something to contribute, regardless of what the tools around them can generate.

The second pillar is Research-Driven Innovation. This is the discipline of grounding ideas in evidence. Real investigation. Competitive analysis. User insight. Data synthesis. AI can accelerate this layer significantly; it can surface patterns, organize information, generate initial hypotheses. But only a designer who knows how to think rigorously about evidence can tell good research from noise. The skill we’re teaching here is not how to search. It’s how to interrogate what you find and build something meaningful from it.

The third pillar is Systems Thinking. This is the ability to see the whole, not just the parts. A brand is not a logo. A digital product is not a screen. A campaign is not an ad. Everything in design exists within a system: typography, color, motion, interaction, content, tone, architecture, experience. A designer who can hold that system in mind at once, who can make a decision about a button and understand how it cascades through every other element, is the designer every studio wants and very few have. AI makes it possible to generate components faster than ever. Systems Thinking is what keeps those components coherent.

The fourth pillar is Domain Knowledge: Informed Intuition and Final Designs. This is the one that relies most heavily on years of developing taste, expertise, and professional judgment. AI can generate options in seconds. The question is: which option is right? That call, aesthetic, conceptual, strategic, is the designer’s. It is what we mean when we talk about art direction. It is the human layer of the workflow that cannot be automated, and it is the most undervalued and underteached capacity in design education. Students who have been trained to consume culture broadly, build expertise deeply, and exercise genuine aesthetic judgment are the ones who can look at a hundred AI-generated options and know which one is worth pursuing, and how to take it somewhere the machine couldn’t.

The fifth pillar is Holistic Creative Intelligence. This is the integrating capacity: the ability to lead a creative project from end to end, across disciplines and collaborators, with clarity of vision and the communication skills to bring others along. It is the capacity to be not just a skilled practitioner but a creative director of your own process. In the age of AI, this pillar has taken on new dimensions. A designer who can orchestrate an AI-assisted workflow, managing the tools, making the judgment calls, maintaining the creative vision throughout, and communicating that vision to a client or team, is operating at a level of creative leadership that the previous generation simply didn’t have to develop as early.

These five capacities are not new. Great designers have always possessed them. What’s changed is that AI has made them the differentiators. When the tool can do the production work, what remains is your ability to think with these capacities at the center. That is what protects your position, your value, your irreplaceable contribution.

That’s the philosophy the curriculum is built on.

Building the actual program was our work together. We had been co-chairing the Creative Technology & Design curriculum at FIT, and we had already been thinking about how to structure a minor program that gave students genuine depth, not just a sampling of electives. The AI-Assisted Design Minor, approved in 2025 and running now, was the culmination of that thinking applied to the most urgent question in design education today. Officially, it’s a minor. In practice, we talk about it as a concentration, because our students are BFA majors first, and this is how they build AI fluency inside that major, not as an elective bolted onto the side of it.

The minor sits within a broader ecosystem of six specializations under the Creative Technology & Design program. Brand Design. Digital Typography. Animation & Motion Design. UX/UI Design. Creative Technology. And AI-Assisted Design. Each minor is built around a specific domain of professional practice. Together they form what we think of as a complete map of where the creative field is going: motion and systems, brand and experience, game and interface, technology and intelligence. Students can specialize, but the design thinking that runs through all of them is the same.

The course that has become the real laboratory for everything I’m describing is CT380: AI-Assisted Design. This is where students learn to work with AI not as a shortcut but as a creative partner. The course is built on what I call a logic-first philosophy: students have to understand the architecture of what they’re building before they reach for a tool. They have to be able to articulate a design decision in words before they can prompt an AI to execute it. That requirement, to think before you generate, is the most important discipline I teach, and the one that makes the biggest difference in the quality of what students produce.

CT390, Rapid Prototyping with AI, launches Fall 2026. It goes further: students use vibe coding workflows to build functional digital products, using AI-native development environments to focus on system architecture and user experience rather than syntax. This course came directly out of something I kept observing in professional practice: designers who could think in terms of systems and logic were able to move from concept to working prototype faster than anyone expected. The barrier to building was no longer technical fluency. It was strategic clarity. CT390 is built to develop that clarity.

CT400, Digital Product Design with AI, is where students bring everything together: concept, research, prototyping, and the final execution of a polished digital product. Students define their own workflow and create the tools that support their creative process. This is the capstone course in many ways. The Five Pillars have to operate simultaneously, under pressure, in a professional context.

Redesigning the courses was one thing. Redesigning how we assess them was something else entirely.

The challenge AI posed to traditional grading was something I began grappling with as soon as I started integrating these tools seriously into CT380. The premise of artifact-based assessment: you judge the quality of what a student produces. It assumes that the artifact is evidence of the student’s thinking. If a student turns in a beautiful brand identity, the logic goes, they must have thought hard and worked well to arrive at it.

AI broke that assumption. Students could generate good enough artifacts in minutes. The artifact no longer proved anything about the process behind it. And the process and iterations, the part that actually demonstrated learning, judgment, and growing design intelligence, remained invisible.

I started saying it this way to colleagues: we’re grading the painting, not the painter.

The framework I’ve been developing to fix this is called Cognitive-Process Assessment, or CPA. The idea is straightforward: assess the thinking, not just the output. Eight dimensions, each of which captures something specific about the intellectual work that happens in an AI-assisted design process.

The first is Prompt Architecture: the strategic thinking behind the instructions a student gives an AI. Good prompt architecture reveals conceptual clarity, intentionality, and the ability to translate a design vision into precise, directive language. I can look at a student’s prompt log and learn more about their intentionally critical thinking process than I can from looking at the final image.

The second is Orchestration Logic: how a student structures and sequences the AI-assisted workflow. Do they know which steps require AI and which require human judgment? Do they make coherent decisions about where to intervene, iterate, redirect? Orchestration is a skill, one that students develop over time and that is entirely legible when you ask them to document it.

The third is Strategic and Content Development: the critical thinking behind the content decisions themselves, feature scope, priorities, value proposition, grounded in research rather than a default or generic choice. This is where I can tell whether a student made a decision or simply accepted the first plausible answer.

The fourth is Critical Curation: how a student evaluates, stress-tests, and refines AI-generated output against real constraints. I ask students to name what they’re catching: an Attribution Error, an unsupported or fabricated claim, versus a Logical Error, a flawed deduction even from a true premise. That distinction alone tells me whether a student is reading AI output critically or just skimming it.

The fifth is Creative Art Direction: the unique aesthetic and conceptual choices that make a student’s work distinctly theirs. In an AI-assisted workflow, this is the human signature. It is what separates a student who is prompting strategically and directing with taste from one who is accepting the first output and calling it done.

The sixth is Domain Specific Knowledge Application: the deep visual communication craft, typography, hierarchy, color, composition, applied skillfully enough to lift AI-assisted work out of the generic and into something original and intentional. This is where years of design training either show up or don’t.

The seventh is Reflective Reasoning: the student’s ability to articulate their own thinking. Can they explain why they made the choices they made? Can they connect those choices to the design brief, to their research, to the Five Pillars? Reflection is not a soft add-on. It is the most reliable evidence of genuine understanding we have.

The eighth is Direct Creative Contribution: the actual, human-made part of the work, an original sketch, a hand-finished detail, an edit that turned an AI draft into something finished. This is the dimension that makes the fingerprint protectable as IP in the clearest sense: not just a record of judgment, but an artifact of authorship.

Together, these eight dimensions produce what I’ve been calling an intellectual fingerprint: a legible record of the student’s original contribution to the work, independent of what AI generated along the way. The intellectual fingerprint is what protects the student’s authorship. It is what validates their creative process as their own. And it is what equips them to walk into a professional context and defend their work with confidence.

The Google Higher Ed Faculty AI Fellowship I was accepted into in 2026, as part of the inaugural North American cohort, has given me the resources and the community to develop CPA further. My challenge statement to the cohort, in its final form, asks how we build a Cognitive-Process Assessment framework, using Google Stitch, Nano Banana (Gemini’s image generation model), Gemini Gems, and NotebookLM. That framework has to capture the domain expertise UX/UI designers apply when directing, writing, and refining AI-assisted design. It has to make the invisible intellectual fingerprint of expert design judgment verifiable, assessable, and protectable as student IP. And it has to scale as the global standard for design higher education by 2027.

The core tension is this: a generalist can prompt Stitch and get a polished-looking UI in seconds. What generic prompting can’t do is direct that process with expertise: iterating with intention across research, content, wireframes, and UI, catching what the AI gets wrong about interaction patterns, cognitive load, accessibility, component scalability. That expert direction, visible across every iteration, is the intellectual fingerprint. CPA makes it visible and assessable.

The intervention, in one line: a Digital Product Design workflow where students act as art director, copywriter, and critical-thinking decision-maker across Stitch, Nano Banana, NotebookLM, Gemini, and Gemini Gems. Students start working with AI from the beginning, for research, content development, wireframing, UI design, and rapid prototyping, iterating heavily while keeping humans in the loop at every decision point. This is a creative workflow built with AI-assisted design, not an evaluation of AI output alone: CPA grades both the student’s decision-making and their own direct contribution to the work. Students verify and timestamp that judgment through periodic in-semester presentations where they explain what they decided and why. CPA is the rubric that grades that record.

None of this is hypothetical. The workflow and the CPA rubric are a roadmap built by analyzing FIT’s own award-winning AI-assisted student work, the same 2026 cohort that took home nine AI-category wins and a Gold Cube from the Art Director’s Club at the One Club Young Ones (twenty-one awards total), and saw more than a third of its sixteen CT380 students hired before graduation. Those outcomes are the pilot. CPA reverse-engineers the pattern of human decisions, the art direction, the copywriting, the critical catches, that produced them, then codifies that pattern into a rubric and a verification mechanism other programs can adopt.

I’m building rubric templates that any educator can adapt. The goal, as I’ve stated it to the fellowship: this isn’t a FIT solution. It’s meant to be the global standard for design higher education by 2027.

The curriculum we’ve described is strong on its own. But curriculum alone has never been enough. The gap we identified in Chapter 1 is not just a gap in what’s taught. It’s a gap between the classroom and the actual creative workforce. Closing it requires building real connections to that workforce: not as a guest speaker series, not as a field trip, but as a structural part of how students learn.

That’s why every major partnership we’ve built is designed to function as living curriculum.

Figma has been the most formative of those partnerships. I’ve been a founding member of the Figma Educator Advisory Board since 2021, and I lead the Friends of Figma Education community; C.J. leads Friends of Figma Game Design. The community we’ve built through those roles has been one of the most rewarding parts of this work. Together, we co-hosted numerous events, including the “What Are You Making with AI?” roundtable at Config 2026. In the past year alone, that list includes “AI-Assisted Design Workflow: Agentic Design with Claude & Figma,” “The Age of CI: The Rise of Creative Intelligence in the Age of AI” panel discussion and exhibition, “Architecting Creativity: Crafting Stories & Ideas, Building with AI,” “The Age of CI: AI Hackathon 2025,” and the FigmaMake Campus Jam: Learning, Making, Innovating Together.² What Figma offers isn’t just software access. It’s a window into where professional design practice is going, a conversation partner on tool development, and a direct line between our students and the platform that most of the industry is using. When students see that the tools they’re learning are being shaped in part by input from their own educators, it changes how they think about their relationship to the field.

Google has been central to the latest phase of the work. Beyond the Fellowship, FIT has become what we describe as a dedicated Google Campus: Gemini, NotebookLM, and Google Classroom are woven into our workflows in ways that go far beyond standard educational licensing. Students aren’t just using Google tools. They’re learning to use them at a professional level, in the same ways that creative teams at studios and agencies are using them.

ElevenLabs came in through our initiative; we’ve developed it jointly because conversational UI became more mainstream and voice AI is one of the most underexplored creative dimensions in design education. The partnership gives students access to an educational program that lets them experiment with voice AI as a creative tool.

FLORA and Reve, two creative startups, provide educational program access that gives students early exposure to tools that are still defining what AI-Assisted Design looks like at the frontier. Adobe’s relationship goes back to 2019, growing out of conversations through the AIGA x Adobe Faculty Council, and we stay actively engaged as design education continues to evolve.

Each of these partnerships was built the same way: by identifying where the gap was between what students needed to know and what the standard curriculum could offer, and then finding the industry partner who could help close it. The relationships are not transactional. They are collaborative, ongoing, and built around the same conviction: that the best thing we can do for the next generation of creative professionals is to put them in real contact with the field they’re entering.

The last piece of the curriculum model, and the most unusual part of it, is the commitment to building in public.

In October 2025, we ran three events in a single week, each one distinct, each one part of the same public argument about what AI-Assisted Design education can look like.

The first was “The Age of Creative Intelligence,” a panel and exhibition that sold out at 250 registrations before the event even arrived. It brought together industry voices, student work, and a live public conversation about where AI is taking the creative field. The energy in that room told us something we’d suspected but now knew: the appetite for this conversation is enormous, and design education has been slow to meet it.

The second was the FigmaMake Campus Jam, held the following day. Students worked live, in a creative environment built around the tools and workflows they’d been learning, alongside mentors from the industry. It was not a showcase. It was a working session, and watching students move with confidence and fluency through an AI-assisted design process, in public, in front of professionals, was one of the most clarifying moments we’ve had as educators.

The third was the semesterly AI Hackathon, the CT380 midterm: students presented a very compelling storytelling project based on the hackathon brief. The standard was high. The work reflected it.

Events like these serve a function that curriculum alone cannot. They create accountability: if you’ve announced something publicly, you have to deliver it. They create visibility for students, for the program, for the ideas. They create connections: industry professionals who attend as judges or mentors become potential employers, collaborators, and partners. And they create a record: evidence, in public, that this kind of education is possible and that it produces extraordinary results.

Building in public has been one of the defining strategies of everything we’ve built over the past several years. ExperienceDesign.NYC. The Creative Intelligence Collective. Design Portfolio Center. These aren’t just websites or platforms. They’re a commitment to doing this work visibly, so that others can learn from it, replicate it, and build on it.

That is the model. Framework first. Program built from the framework. Assessment designed to measure what the framework demands. Industry woven in as curriculum, not added on as a supplement. And everything made visible, so the work can travel beyond the walls of any single institution.

What it produces is something we couldn’t have fully predicted when we started building it: designers who feel genuinely empowered. Who don’t approach AI with anxiety or skepticism, but with the confidence that comes from understanding what they bring to the work that AI cannot. Who direct the tools instead of deferring to them, and who lead every project like it’s theirs to shape, because it is.

The next chapter is theirs.
