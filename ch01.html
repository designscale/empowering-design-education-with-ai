Chapter 1: The Gap

“The purpose of education is to replace an empty mind with an open one.” Malcolm S. Forbes

“The history of design is a story of transformation. Our tools evolve: manual becomes mechanical, mechanical becomes digital, and digital becomes intelligent. Yet through every shift, our essence remains the same: empathy, imagination, and the passion to create meaning. We don’t chase change; we shape it. AI is removing the friction of production, giving us the freedom to focus on what truly defines us: our ability to envision, to innovate, and to create with uniquely human Creative Intelligence.” C.J. Yeh & Christie Shin

We are living through the most exciting moment in the history of design education. I want to say that clearly, at the outset, because this book is not a warning. It is an invitation.

At my core, I am a designer. That has been true for over thirty years, and it remains the foundation of everything I do. Design is not what I studied. It is how I see the world, how I solve problems, how I contribute. It is my profession and my passion.

Teaching came from that same place. I didn’t enter academia to step away from practice. I entered it because I wanted to share what I’d learned, to give young creatives the knowledge, the tools, and the confidence to build careers as strong as the ones I’d seen possible. I am a professor at the Fashion Institute of Technology in New York, where I teach AI-Assisted Design, digital product design, and the creative systems that define how designers work today. And I am a design director and partner at CML (Cynda Media Lab), a studio whose clients have included Google, MIT, Starbucks, Target, the NFL, and the Girl Scouts USA. For me, practice and teaching have always been the same commitment: to design, and to the people who will carry it forward.

My co-author and collaborator C.J. Yeh shares this exact orientation. He founded CML; I joined as his partner over a decade ago, because we saw the same things and wanted to build the same things. As designers, we are driven by craft, by conceptual rigor, by the belief that design has the power to shape culture. C.J. has been teaching since 2003, and together we co-chair the Creative Technology & Design curriculum at FIT. As educators, we are driven by something equally clear: the conviction that the best thing we can do for the next generation of designers is to refuse to let academic convention stand between them and the knowledge they actually need. That shared vision is why we run the studio together. It is also why we wrote this book together.

That belief is what brought us to AI before most of our academic peers were ready to take it seriously. At CML, we began integrating AI tools into real client work: a full rebranding, website, and marketing campaign for Vibrant Health, along with a growing number of other client projects. Working with AI as a creative companion changed how the work felt. We were the orchestrators. Every strategic decision, every aesthetic call, every directional choice remained ours. What changed was capacity. We could explore more directions, develop deeper concepts, pursue bolder ideas. The creative vision was still entirely human. AI amplified what we could do with it.

That experience made something clear very quickly: this wasn’t a tool to evaluate from a distance. It was already reshaping professional practice. And if it was reshaping practice, it had to reshape how we taught.

That sense of urgency, not fear but possibility pressing against a timeline, is what set everything in motion. Because here is the honest challenge: the creative industry is moving at a pace that the traditional academic curriculum was never designed to match. The tools, workflows, and ways of thinking that define professional practice today are evolving faster than curriculum committees can convene, faster than approval cycles can accommodate, faster than textbooks can be written. AI has not created this gap between education and industry, but it has made it impossible to ignore.

The architecture of the university curriculum was not designed for a world moving at the speed of AI. Curriculum development cycles can take two years or longer. That pace no longer works for our students.

The World Economic Forum estimates that 42% of business tasks will be automated by 2027.¹ The cognitive layer, the strategic layer, the layer where human judgment and creative intelligence live. That’s what remains. But the real skills required to operate at that layer, to direct AI rather than be displaced by it, to orchestrate rather than merely execute, are changing faster than any curriculum committee can convene. We are, as a field, perpetually teaching to an industry that has already moved on.

Figma’s 2026 AI report puts a number on how fast this is moving inside the very studios and product teams our students are graduating into. In a single year, the share of developers doing design work jumped from 44% to 60%, and the share of designers participating in development nearly doubled, from 21% to 41%.² The old handoff, design finishes a file, hands it to engineering, is dissolving in real time. Forty-one percent of product builders now say AI has significantly changed how their team works together, six times as many as said so two years earlier. That is not a five-year trend. That is last year.

None of this has made design less important. It has made it more important, and that is the part we most want educators to sit with. Ninety percent of people building AI products say design matters at least as much as it did before AI, and 57% say it matters more, a figure that climbs to 65% among developers themselves.³ Ask practitioners what the next few years demand, and the answer is close to unanimous: 91% of designers, 96% of developers, and 96% of product managers say AI fluency will be essential to their future success. Our students are not being asked to choose between craft and AI fluency. They are being told, by the people doing the hiring, that both are now table stakes.

We’ve spent our entire academic careers trying to close that gap. The goal has never been to replace rigorous curriculum with pure practicality; it’s to bridge the two. Strong foundational knowledge matters. Conceptual depth matters. But so does the ability to apply that knowledge in real conditions, with the tools and workflows that the creative workforce actually uses. The question we’ve always asked when designing a course is: how do we build curriculum that honors both, one that gives students the intellectual foundation and the applied fluency to walk into a studio on their first day and contribute?

That balance between knowledge and its application has driven every course we’ve built and every partnership we’ve sought.

It’s also what brought us to AI-Assisted Design before most of our colleagues in academia had begun to take it seriously.

Because we were using these tools. We were watching our industry peers use them. We were seeing, in real time, the shift happening in professional practice: the way AI was changing not just what designers craft, but how they conceptually approach the work, how they operate, how they move from an idea to its execution. And we knew, with absolute certainty, that our students needed to understand this shift before they graduated. Not after. Not during some painful on-the-job adjustment period where they felt behind and scrambled to catch up. Before.

Students should be workforce-ready at graduation. That has always been our north star. The creative industry does not slow down while new graduates find their footing. The studios, the agencies, the in-house teams that hire our students need people who can contribute from day one. The cost of a graduate who spends six months learning on the job what they should have learned in school is not just a cost to the graduate. It’s a cost to the employer, and ultimately, a cost to the credibility of design education itself.

There’s a version of this conversation that is entirely about tools: which AI platforms to use, how to integrate them into existing courses, how to teach prompting technique and generative workflows. That conversation is important. There are also real concerns that deserve serious attention: the misuse of AI, questions of authorship and academic integrity, ethical responsibility in how these tools are developed and deployed. Those conversations matter too. But none of them is the one we’re trying to have in this book.

What we’re trying to have is a deeper conversation about what design education is actually for in the age of Creative Intelligence.

Design reflects life. It reflects human behavior, society, evolving needs, and the technologies that shape how we interact with the world. When we think about what our students need to be able to do, not just with AI but in the field, as professionals, over the course of a career, we come back to a set of core capacities that have nothing to do with any specific tool. The ability to frame a problem with empathy. The discipline to ground ideas in real research. The systems thinking to hold an entire design ecosystem in mind at once. The domain knowledge and aesthetic judgment to know when the work is right. The holistic intelligence to lead a project from concept to completion and communicate that vision throughout.

These are the capacities that define a great designer. They are also, we’ve come to believe, the capacities that AI cannot replace, but can profoundly amplify, if students are taught to use it that way.

AI is not a shortcut. It’s a lens. It sharpens your vision without replacing your eye. You still decide what to look at, what matters, what to make. AI just gives you a wider, more powerful way to see and to execute on that vision.

The designers who will thrive in this era are not the ones who use AI to generate something faster and easier. They’re the ones who use AI to think bigger, who feel genuinely empowered by these tools, who direct them with intention and intelligence, who see themselves not as operators of technology but as architects of creative vision.

That is what we are trying to teach. That is what this book is about.

What follows is both a first-person account and a field guide. It is the story of how we built a curriculum, a program, an ecosystem of industry partnerships and public events and student opportunities that tries to close the gap we’ve been describing. A program that integrates AI not as a novelty or an add-on but as a fundamental dimension of how designers think and work. A program whose students walked into the 2026 One Club Young Ones competition and came out with twenty-one awards, including a Gold Cube from the Art Director’s Club and nine wins in categories built specifically for AI-assisted work, work that, two years ago, no design student could have made.

But it is also a replicable framework. The gap we’re describing is not FIT’s gap. It belongs to every institution that is trying to prepare creative professionals for a workforce that is moving faster than curriculum committees can keep up with. The structural lag we’ve experienced is not unique to our context; it is endemic to higher education. The philosophy, the partnerships, the methods we’ve developed, and the proof of what’s possible when you move fast and build in public, can all be adapted, adopted, and made your own.

This is not a book about what AI can do. There are plenty of those.

This is a book about what design educators can do, right now, without waiting for permission, to make sure that the students sitting in their classrooms today are ready for the industry that will be waiting for them when they graduate. And it is a book for early-stage creatives and designers who want to upskill, advance their careers, and lead with Creative Intelligence in a field that is moving fast.

The gap is real. The urgency is real. And the work of closing it starts in the classroom, with us.
