Part IV: Field Guide

The Replicable Framework

“The future of design education belongs to the ‘orchestrators,’ those who can leverage AI to bridge the gap between imagination and execution.” Christie Shin

Everything up to this point has been an argument, and evidence. The gap is real (Chapter 1). Here is a framework built to close it (Chapter 2). Here is the proof that the framework works (Chapter 3). Here is confirmation, from outside the classroom, that the field wants exactly what the framework produces (Chapter 4).

This chapter is different. It’s not trying to convince you of anything. It’s for the educator who is already convinced, who has already decided that waiting for a curriculum committee to catch up is not a plan, and who wants to know: what do I actually do on Monday?

I’m going to tell you. Not as theory. As the actual sequence of decisions C.J. and I made, in the order we made them, so you can adapt them to whatever institution, discipline, or resource level you’re working with. None of what follows requires FIT’s budget, FIT’s industry relationships, or FIT’s timeline. It requires a willingness to build before you have permission, and to make what you build visible while you’re still building it.

Start With Assessment, Not Curriculum

Here is the mistake I see most often when educators try to bring AI into their teaching: they start with content. They add a unit, a workshop, a guest lecture on prompting. That’s a reasonable first step, but it doesn’t survive contact with the actual problem, which is that the artifact your students hand in no longer proves what it used to prove.

Start where I started: with the assessment question. Before you decide what to teach, decide how you’ll know whether a student actually learned it, in a world where the final deliverable can be generated in minutes by someone who understood nothing about how it got there.

That’s what the Cognitive-Process Assessment framework, or CPA, is built to solve. I described the full philosophy behind it in Chapter 2, so I won’t repeat it here. What I want to give you in this chapter is the adaptable version: the part any educator, in any discipline, can pick up and use this semester.

CPA grades five dimensions of a student’s process, not the final output:

Prompt Architecture. Have students keep a prompt log. Not a polished document. A running record of what they asked for, in what order, and why. You will learn more about a student’s conceptual clarity from that log than from the artifact it produced.

Orchestration Logic. Ask students to document their workflow: which steps they handed to AI, which steps they kept for themselves, and why they drew the line where they drew it. This is a teachable skill, and once you ask students to make it explicit, you’ll find most of them have never been asked to think about it before.

Critical Curation. Require a revision trail. What did the AI generate first, what did the student keep, what did they discard, and what’s their stated reason. This is where you’ll find out whether a student has taste, or is simply accepting whatever came out first.

Creative Art Direction. Ask for an annotated version of the final piece: what’s the student’s, specifically, and what did they build on top of a generated starting point. This protects authorship, and it forces students to articulate their own contribution instead of assuming it’s self-evident.

Reflective Reasoning. End with a short written or verbal reflection: why these choices, connected back to the brief. This is the least novel part of the framework and the most reliably diagnostic. If a student can’t explain their own decisions, the work wasn’t really theirs.

You do not need Stitch, Nano Banana, or NotebookLM to run this. Those are the Google tools I’m using inside the Fellowship to build a version of this specifically for UX and UI, because the Fellowship’s challenge is scoped to interface design. But the five dimensions above are tool-agnostic. Run them with whatever your students are already using. The framework is the point, not the platform.

Start small. Pick one assignment this semester and build a CPA rubric for it instead of your usual grading criteria. You will find, almost immediately, that you’re having better conversations with your students about their work, because you’re finally asking about the part that actually required them.

Build the Toolkit, Not Just the Course

CPA solves the assessment problem for one course, taught by one instructor. The next step is turning it into something that scales past you.

That’s the second half of my Google Higher Ed Faculty AI Fellowship challenge, and it’s the part I think matters most for readers of this book: the AI Pedagogy Toolkit. As I write this, I’m still inside the fellowship. The Kickoff happened in June. Two more working sessions and an in-person presentation at Google’s Mountain View campus are still ahead of me this fall, and I’ll have more to report on where the toolkit lands by the time this book is in your hands. But the shape of it is already clear, and you don’t need to wait for my final version to start building your own.

The toolkit has five components, and I’d encourage any educator building this for their own program to build the same five, in roughly this order:

Rubric templates, adaptable across disciplines, not just UX and UI. Once you’ve built one CPA rubric for one assignment, the template does most of the work for the next one.

Tool-specific workflow guides. Mine are built around the Google toolkit, because that’s what the Fellowship scoped me into: Stitch for interface generation, Nano Banana for visual assets, NotebookLM for turning research into a brief. Yours might be built around Figma, Midjourney, Claude, whatever your program has already standardized on. The point isn’t the specific tools. It’s writing down, once, clearly, how research becomes a brief becomes a generated draft becomes a directed final piece, so the next instructor in your department doesn’t have to reinvent that sequence from scratch.

Process portfolio templates, so students have a consistent structure for capturing prompt logs, orchestration decisions, and revision trails, instead of improvising documentation from assignment to assignment.

Faculty onboarding curriculum. This is the piece programs skip, and it’s the piece that actually determines whether any of this survives past the person who built it. If only one instructor understands CPA, you have a class, not a program. Write down how you’d teach another faculty member to run this in a semester.

A public version of all of it. Not locked in a shared drive only your department can access. My own scaling path is explicit: FIT to SUNY to any design program that wants it. I’ve said this directly to the Fellowship cohort and I’ll say it here: this isn’t a FIT solution. It’s meant to be the global standard for any program where AI has made the artifact an unreliable measure of skill. That only happens if the toolkit is actually shareable.

Build Infrastructure the Institution Doesn’t Own

Here is the part of this chapter that has nothing to do with Google, Figma, or any fellowship, and everything to do with a decision C.J. and I made years ago: don’t wait for the institution to build the platform. Build your own, alongside it.

ExperienceDesign.NYC is ours. The Creative Intelligence Collective is ours. Design Portfolio Center, which we co-founded in 2024, is ours. None of these belong to FIT. They exist because C.J. and I decided that the work of closing the gap between education and industry couldn’t be confined to a single institution’s timeline, budget, or approval process. A curriculum proposal takes two years to move through committee. A website takes a weekend.

This matters for a reason beyond speed. It means the framework, the events, the industry relationships, and yes, this book, are portable. If I moved institutions tomorrow, the infrastructure would move with me. That’s not a contingency plan. It’s the whole point. The goal was never to build something that only works inside FIT’s walls. It was to build something that proves the model, so that any educator reading this can build their own version of it, inside or alongside whatever institution they teach at.

If you’re starting from nothing, here’s the honest order of operations. Build the course first, because that’s where your credibility comes from. Build the assessment framework second, because that’s what makes the course defensible and the results legible. Then build a public home for the work, even a simple one, because a framework that only exists in your syllabus doesn’t travel. It doesn’t have to be elaborate. It has to exist, and it has to be yours.

Why Building in Public Actually Matters

I described the Age of Creative Intelligence week, the panel that sold out at 250 registrations, the FigmaMake Campus Jam, the AI Hackathon, in Chapter 2, as evidence of what this ecosystem produces. I want to close this chapter by telling you why I think it’s not optional.

A framework you keep private stays a private opinion. A framework you put in front of an audience, with your name on it, with students presenting live, with industry professionals in the room as judges and mentors, becomes something you’re accountable to. That accountability is not a burden. It’s the mechanism that keeps the work honest and keeps it moving.

It also does something curriculum development alone cannot: it builds the relationships that make everything else in this book possible. Every industry partner named in Chapter 2, every voice you heard from in Chapter 4, is a relationship that started at an event, a roundtable, a portfolio review night, not a cold outreach email. Figma’s Educator Advisory Board. The Config roundtables. Thirteen events across four years, in New York, San Francisco, Seoul, and Singapore. The same names recur across those events, not because the field is small, but because sustained visibility builds sustained relationships, and sustained relationships are what let you keep a curriculum current in a field that changes every semester.

If you take one thing from this chapter and nothing else, take this: you do not need permission to start. You need a course, a rubric, and a willingness to show your work before it’s finished. Everything else, the partnerships, the toolkit, the recognition, is downstream of that first decision to build in public instead of waiting to build in private until it’s perfect.

It’s never going to be perfect. Mine isn’t. I’m still inside this fellowship, still refining CPA, still waiting on the last few interviews for the chapter before this one. I’m publishing the process anyway, because the process is the point.

Build the thing. Show the thing. Let the field tell you what to fix.

That’s the whole method. Everything else in this book is just what it looks like when you actually do it.
