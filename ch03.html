Chapter 3: The Design Students

“Creativity still comes from the creator.” Elias Cherrier-Vickers, CT380 student

Arguments are easy. Frameworks are necessary. But neither one is proof.

Our forward thinking curriculum works. This chapter is the proof.

In 2026, students from our program won twenty-one awards at the One Club Young Ones competition, nine of them in newly created AI categories. One of those students won a Gold Cube from the Art Director’s Club, in the AI: Process category. That is one of the most prestigious student design awards given anywhere in the world. It went to a designer who, two years ago, would not have had the tools, the framework, or the confidence to make the work that won it.

That number, twenty-one awards, is not a statistic we wave around because it sounds impressive. It is evidence of something specific: that students who have been taught to direct AI with intention and intelligence, who have been given a framework for thinking rather than just a toolkit for executing, produce work at a level that the field recognizes as exceptional.

Let us show you what that looks like.

The project that defines this cohort, the one we keep coming back to when we want to explain what Creative Intelligence actually produces, is Bunny Quest.

Kokona Akatsu and Haein Noh began this project in CT300, Kinetic Typography, taught by C.J. I then took them on as a mentor, and together we pushed the concept further. From there, they continued into the CT&D Creative Fellowship for deeper development, where Bunny Quest fully came to life. The brief asked them to think about how FIT’s Creative Technology & Design minor programs could better connect with prospective students, particularly Gen Z, for whom traditional academic promotional materials, the brochures and program pages and dry course descriptions, read as exactly what they are: institutional communication that doesn’t speak their language.

Their answer was not to update the brochure. It was to throw it out entirely and replace it with something the target audience would actually engage with. They personified each of the six CT&D minors as a unique bunny character: approachable, expressive, and designed with a distinct visual personality that made each program feel like something you’d want to be part of. They developed a full cast of characters, with posters, stickers, and social media personalities that brought each minor to life in a completely different register than anything FIT had produced before.

And then they built an AI-powered quiz game that let prospective students discover which bunny, which minor, which creative path, was their match.

What strikes us every time we look at this project is not just the quality of the execution, though the execution is extraordinary. It’s the completeness of the thinking. Problem Framing: they understood that the real challenge wasn’t design visibility, it was emotional relevance. Research-Driven Innovation: they studied how Gen Z actually engages with brand characters and interactive content. Systems Thinking: every bunny, every color palette, every piece of content exists within a coherent visual system that still allows for individual personality. Domain Knowledge: the aesthetic judgments throughout, from character design to motion to copy, are precise and intentional. Holistic Creative Intelligence: they built an end-to-end experience that worked as a campaign, as a game, as a social presence, and as a piece of design that told a clear and compelling story.

When the Gold Cube was announced, we felt something beyond pride. It was validation: of the mentorship, of the art direction, of the belief that students rise to the level of what is asked of them.

Bunny Quest is the headline. But it is not an outlier. What we saw across the 2026 cohort, again and again, was students who had internalized something deeper than technique: the ability to use AI as a genuine creative partner without losing themselves in the process.

Vashtie Persaud and Thalia Merino made Clash of Netflix, a campaign that started with a sharp cultural insight: stress relief is universal, and audiences under pressure retreat to the familiar. Seventy-five percent of players turn to gaming; fifty-five percent turn to TV. The brief they set for themselves was audacious: how do you make Clash of Clans the number one show on Netflix before it even exists? Their answer was an invasion. Clash of Clans infiltrated Netflix’s biggest fandoms, placing elements that felt familiar but slightly off, slightly wrong, until Netflix “launched a counterattack” and introduced Clash of Netflix as its own property. The campaign used AI throughout: to generate imagery, to create cross-brand visual mashups, to produce a volume of content that the concept required. But the idea, the cultural insight, the strategic architecture of the campaign, was entirely theirs.

Polina Andreyevna Kalmatckaia made The Thread of Love, a puppet animation that celebrates love in all its forms: romantic, familial, platonic, unconditional. The project uses handcrafted characters and a red thread as a visual motif, weaving through a story about connection across difference. What Polina did with AI was not to generate the animation but to extend her own craft, producing imagery and motion that her hands alone couldn’t have achieved at the scale and quality the concept demanded. The result is a piece of work that is unmistakably personal, unmistakably hers, and made possible by AI without being defined by it.

Elias Cherrier-Vickers made Merboy, a two-minute piece built for Instagram that uses his own journey through Florida’s springs as a way to explore his heritage and identity. He built it using ChatGPT, Reve, ElevenLabs, and After Effects: a real AI-assisted production workflow, handled by a design student who had learned to orchestrate these tools rather than simply operate them. In his project description, Elias wrote something we’ve since quoted in almost every conversation we’ve had about AI in design education: “Creativity still comes from the creator.” That line is everything. It is the entire philosophy of what we’re trying to teach, stated by a student who had earned the right to say it.

Think Gemini came from a four-person team: Vashtie Persaud, Thalia Merino, Elias Cherrier-Vickers, and Hailey Hosang, working in CT400, Digital Product Design with AI. Their brief addressed a real and urgent tension: students increasingly rely on AI for their work, and teachers increasingly distrust it. Think Gemini is a product concept that bridges that gap, giving teachers a workspace where they can set boundaries, provide aligned guidance, and use AI as a tool for genuine learning rather than a shortcut around it. The tagline says it simply: Small Changes. Big Learning. This is a team of design students who looked at one of the most contested questions in education today and designed a product-level solution for it. The irony is not lost on us: students in an AI-Assisted Design course built a tool to help educators feel more confident about AI in learning.

Cecilia Garcia Pizales made two projects that demonstrate range. Bloom Your Way is an integrated AI campaign celebrating each student’s individual path, using AI-generated visuals, video, and an interactive quiz to capture what it actually feels like to be in school, to be figuring out who you are, to be becoming. Cecilia’s other work on the gallery, dibujo.bmp, takes a different register entirely, exploring digital memory and personal archive through AI tools in a way that is more experimental, more intimate, and just as technically accomplished.

Thalia Merino, who appears in both Clash of Netflix and Think Gemini as a collaborator, also produced Legacy.wav as a solo project: an experimental music mixer app with a shareable visualizer, built using Claude for vibe coding. Thalia is the kind of student who makes you understand what CT390, our Rapid Prototyping with AI course launching in Fall 2026, is designed to produce. She didn’t just design Legacy.wav; she built it. The line between designer and developer dissolved entirely in her hands, because she had the strategic clarity and the AI fluency to make it dissolve.

Yuriko Zheng and Joyce Ahn’s Gemini project, also from CT400, addressed the same tension Think Gemini was exploring from a different angle. Their concept: NotebookLM, powered by Google Gemini, makes it possible for the same lesson to become slides, a podcast, or a video, meeting each learner where they are. The tagline is precise: learning was never one size fits all. Two students, looking at Google’s existing tools, and seeing a product story that Google hadn’t yet fully told. That is what Domain Knowledge and Holistic Creative Intelligence produce together: the ability to look at what exists and see what it could become.

Shalika Thappali’s Holi Babies, made during the CT380 AI Hackathon, is a player-versus-environment game built around the childlike joy of the Holi festival. It is playful, culturally specific, and technically realized: the kind of work that shows a student who understands that AI-Assisted Design isn’t just for campaigns and products, but for any creative form where a human has something genuine to say.

What we see across all of this work is not a set of impressive student projects. We see a proof of concept.

Every one of these students came into CT380 or CT400 as a designer who had been trained in traditional craft: typography, composition, brand, communication. None of them came in as technologists. Most of them, if you’d asked them a year before, would not have described themselves as people who could build an interactive quiz game, produce a two-minute AI-generated film, or design a digital product with a functional prototype. The barrier, as we’ve said before, was never logic. It was confidence.

What the curriculum gave them was a framework for understanding where their intelligence lives in an AI-assisted workflow. They learned to frame problems before they reached for tools. They learned to build research into the early stages rather than skipping to execution. They learned to see their work as a system, not a series of individual deliverables. They learned that their aesthetic judgment, their taste, their point of view, is precisely what AI cannot replicate, and that exercising it deliberately and consistently is what separates a designer from a generator.

The One Club Young Ones results are one measure of that. Twenty-one awards in one year, nine of them in AI categories that didn’t exist when most of these students started college. Six out of sixteen students secured employment before graduation, stepping into studios and agencies and in-house teams that needed exactly what they had learned to do.

But the measure we trust most is not the awards. It is the line Elias wrote in his project description, almost as a throwaway, almost as an obvious thing.

Creativity still comes from the creator.

He wrote that because he knew it was true. Because he had done the work to make it true. Because he had spent a semester building something real using AI tools, and had come out the other side knowing, in his body, that everything that mattered in that work had come from him.

That is what we are trying to give every student who walks into our classroom.
